7 [24bvy5zx41tjd] March 16, 2009 at 02:25AM
I am having that debate with myself again as southern rolls around. For me when I started it was the ultimate chance to run away and join the circus for weekends in spring and fall. It was learning a way to express myself and allow myself to both grow up and be the little kid that I always wanted to be. It was people who didn't think I was weird to want to play dress up and play in the faire dirt. It was people who solely judged my on the fact I was willing to play and willing to give of myself. The times listening to injured souls and helped me to heal the damaged parts of my own psyche. It was also friends that were always there - not just a faire, but all through the year. When my parents were dying these people were more there than others that said they were my friends. These are the people I judge other peoples words against their actions. For all that is said at faire, it was the things that faire folk did for each other that spoke louder than words. The hugs and the love that wasn't expecting anything in return. The glass of water and instructions to take a rest and not make you feel you were not pulling your part of the load. The stories that involved great imagination and a flair for the dramatic that inspired me to continue and to giggle like a five year old in the midst of giggle fits. The kinds that brought tears to your eyes and ribs still aching days later. It seems there is more push to have people fit a mold and so many of the greats I have met and known were all out of broken molds. So I wait to see if it is still fun and if I am still there for the people I know and have known over the years. Everything changes - am I inventive enough to keep the fun and free spirited play alive? Maybe that is the question for me this time around.

17 Greig Fors November 30, 2008 at 03:28AM
Hi Luisa, I did watch the video, and it was great. You look soooo young. You are right, the feeling is portrayed very well. It made me want to go back. There were quite a few half remembered faces in the video. Southern was my vacation for many years. I would always take Memorial weekend off and go there. An example of the panoply of knowledge/backgrounds of faire people (and I do resist the urge to label ourselves as faeiries) was the time I was walking around as night security, I stopped at a booth that had a few people playing Irish music. They finished playing and started talking about the song. A question arose about "how do you say that in Gaelic?" At least five people in the audience told them. This has always been one of my memories that impressed me as to how much faire people know. The other quick story is how ingenius we are. A friend worked at Lawrence Livermore and found a state of the art nuclear vessel (a globe shape and not radioactive) and turned it into a thirteenth century mace. Faire kept many people on their toes, both physically and mentally. Not only the knowledge, but the humour as well. There are many, many stories that we can relate across the spool table.
