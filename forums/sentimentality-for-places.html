2 PatriciaBlanco April 01, 2010 at 02:21AM
What a profound relief that the Agoura site was saved. In large part because Art Wizzen and his son died in a plane crash, and with them the plans to raze the site and develop it. From there a fortunate chain of events and interventions led to the site being held in trust, unmolested, for all. Agoura is my home site. I am after all a southern girl. To return last Spring for the reunion, having never before returned after our last day of faire there, was so completely overwhelming that I wept repeatedly. Every tree, every meadow, the smell of sage, the mists wrapping the hills, and then the reconnection with the tribe healed and nurtured me. I do not know what lays in store for me when I return north again. I just read that the red bard was torn down. I didn't know that, funny but I had a dream about that very thing this past week. But the smell of bay laurel always takes me there, as does the sight of rainbow rings around the moon. Full heart.

6 Richard Beard March 31, 2010 at 10:44PM
I was the last of the crew to leave the site. I had lived on site for over thirteen years across from the Gray Barn. Before I left they had cut down an estimated 30,000 trees. They had just started tearing down the Red barn. They turned the Cottage into toothpicks and as for the Faire valley, everything from the road up to the snow fencing was completely gone, scraped clean down to the dirt. Every single tree in Crew Valley was toppled and most on Snob hill as well. It was the most devastating thing I have ever had the misfortune to witness in my life to date. How anyone can call a golf course " Beautiful " after seeing all that had been destroyed, is beyond me. It was a Magical piece of prime forest that is now gone forever. Thank God that there is a small piece left, here and there that might serve as a reminder of what it once was. Those times when I need to go to the City, I avoid Hwy 37. I'd rather remember my home as it was. In my heart it will always be pristine.

9 [1k7yfe86iwp1h] February 25, 2010 at 05:31AM
I develop serious connections to any place where deep emotional action took place. It's usually a bad idea for me to go back to them, because time will always do its thing. I'm generally better off with the pictures in my head. I have vivid dreams of the house I grew up in, but I know if I were to go back and visit it, I would be stunned by the change, and by other people occupying the space that is/was so much a part of what makes me me. Before they built the houses up on the ridge above the Blackpoint site, I went up there and looked down on the golf course, trying to make out the places I'd grown up loving. It was a depressing exercise, but I was oddly grateful that the course preserved the natural beauty rather than having luxury MacMansions built over it. Whenever I pass, I see the Red Barn in spite of the fact that it's no longer there. I can't imagine the hole left for the people who actually lived there.
