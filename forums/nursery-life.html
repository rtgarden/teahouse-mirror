Nursery Life - "Bea Grow's Rose Formula - April"- 4-18-09. Your roses should be starting to bud/bloom by now.Watering becomes important now. Roses really should be watered by hand. It much easier to monitor them individually this way. Water in the morning. Especially near the coast. It gives the leaves that get wet a chance to dry off, thus reducing the chance of powdery mildew. Mild cases should not concern you. In more extreme cases, plucking off leaves may be in order. No, no fungicide sprays. Remember, we're going organic here.Let the plants develop their own defenses.A strong plant is more resistant to disease and pests. And let's feed our hungry, hungry roses some more. Get a bucket and make a solution of seaweed extract and fish emulsion according to packaging directions and feed them all. A rose I'm getting to know this season is "Barbra Streisand". All you violet lovers will love the color of this one. Wonderful fragrance. Large blooms with a high pointed center on long stems.Word is the lady herself made this choice and was very, uh,"exacting" in her selection. It paid off. This will become a real classic.

Nursery life "Snails" - an addition. Snails don't like gypsum. It dries them out too quickly for them to try to cross. If they're attacking a particular plant just surround that plant with a ring of gypsum. It's cheap, it's easy, it's good for your garden. have you ever seen those white rinds around the trunks of citrus trees in an orchard. Yep. Gypsum, Keeps the snails out of the fruit. Of course in Ojai our neighbor Dr. Bee keep a flock of Peacocks. They love snails. Kept his orchard clear of snails. Not a practical solution for the rest of us, but effective.

Nursery life 3-25-09 "Snails, aphids and whiteflies" Recently I saw some discussion on chat regarding snails in the garden. I saw the recommendation of using beer to deal with snails. Well, it is true that snails are attracted to beer. Fill a pan with brew and you'll get a pan full of dead drunk snails. One little problem with this. It REALLY attracts snails. LOTS of snails. It's like a snail party in your garden. Now if you happen to eat snails this might be cool. Beer fattened snails. Yum! (not) Actually a very effective way to deal with snails is the time old method of plucking the pests when you see them, Early morning and early evening when they migrate gather them up and dispose of them. Dispose? Well, my condo fronts on a creek, so I toss them over the fence into the creek. Water birds love them. Live on a street? Ah, nothing like the satisfying crunch of the snail landing on the asphalt. I read of a normally sweet natured old auntie who gathered them up and gleefully incinerated them. Actually if you just keep up the plucking you can keep your garden remarkably snail free. Snail bait is so very expensive, and can be dangerous to small critters. My friend uses Deadline with some success. Sluggo and "It's it" are also very good. For those going nuclear, Methaldahide (sp?) is a very dangerous, ultra effective method. Don't do it. Really. Don't. Now to my favorite pest determent. Worm poop. Yep, worm poop. Sold at many nurseries as "Worm Glow' or"Worm Gold". Very effective for the repelling of aphids and white flies. When the worm poop gets into the system of the plant it makes it taste nasty to pests. A really good control on food crops. Safe and, for those to whom such things are important, vegan. Lay the poo on top of the soil under your plant about 1-2 inches thick and water. Not smelly. Dislodging aphids and whitefly with a hard spray of water works well. If whitefly has started sticking to the undersides of the plant pluck off those leaves. That's how they lay eggs. Ladybugs! You've seen them for sale in nurseries, or heard of using them. 2 things, 1- use correctly. You can't just let them go in your garden,they'll fly away. They feed in the early morning and early evening. Spray your foliage first (they like moist) then shake them about the plants that host pests (roses maybe?)They can be a little sluggish at first, no worries, they'll wake up. 2 - If you use any kind of chemical insecticide it will kill the ladybugs as well as any other beneficial insects. Start this integrated pest management now. Prevention is your best strategy.

Nursery Life - 2-16-09 " Succulent Basket" - I recently attended a San Clemente garden club meeting because the guest speaker was Debra Lee Baldwin, author of one of my favorite garden books "Designing with Succulents". She showed a photo of a succulent basket and described it's creation. The next day at the nursery I created one, see photo of it on my page. You start with a wire basket about 12" or so in diameter. Next you get a ground cover flat of a well established Sedum. A ground cover flat is a flat of dirt into which cuttings have been planted directly, no separate pots.I used Sedum Angelicum, other suitable Sedums are, Brevifolia, or Spurium.Then you flip the flat of Sedum face down into the basket. This creates a living liner for the basket.Make sure you select a fully filled & established flat. Next fill in with enough soil to support the gallon sized focus plant (nursery gallons are not true gallons, they are smaller, which is why women make better nurserymen than carpenters, sorry, old joke).The top of the soil of the focus plant should be even with the top of the basket. Back fill with potting soil.Plant small succulents into top soil around focus plant. Nestle some into the wall of the basket. We also used a couple of Alpine strawberries in the walls. Pretty & edible. Now bear in mind, this basket will be HEAVY. You will need chains to hang it. We just set it into a cast iron pot holder till we could get the chains. It sold before we got that far. Be creative! My next succulent basket will explore the orange, yellow, red succulents.

Nursery Life - 1-28-09 - "On Cleaning Vines...". I've been spending many hours in our vine section these past 2 weeks. They get overgrown and full of dead leaves. I start by clipping the ties that affix them to their stakes. I really dislike wire ties.They cut into the vines as they grow, much better is plastic plant tie tape. It gives as the vine grows.Next I untangle the growth, fingers flying through the tangles, dislodging dry leaves, dust, and twigs. After trimming the vines I retie them, holding the vines to the stake and working the tie tape around twice and tying off with a square knot. As I'm working I realize - This is really easy for me, it's as if I had muscular memory - Then it hits me, of course!, the faire! Waking up on Sunday mornings at faire after sleeping in sawdust and hay. Having no comb in my pocket (only a mangled Night Pass), I rake my fingers through my tangled hair, dislodging straw, hay and sawdust. After twisting a quick braid I use a piece of ribbon to tie off my braid(wrap twice around, finish with a square knot) and hasten off to my morning dance set. Who would have guessed that that particular set of skills would have become so useful after all these years!

Nursery life 1-7-09, "Baby it's cold out there" Back to the nursery and the first thing is dealing with the cold damage. This brings to mind,what to do to mitigate damage.Cold damage happens, but we can do things to reduce damage. Sure, it would be nice to just heat up the area, but smudge pots are a no-no.Keeping a supply of nursery cloth to insulate your plants would be nice, but bulky to store and costly and difficult to procure. Use what you have on hand. The most simple thing is to just spread sheets of newspaper over sensitive plants. It's cheap, easy, and recyclable. You can also use old sheets, those foil-like space blankets, mesquito netting, plastic,just try to provide a little insulation. Don't forget to remove it in the morning after the temp raises a bit. Making teepees helps taller shrubs. Form a tripod of sticks bound at the top. Drape with one the previous mentioned covers.Now lets say your plants have already been smacked by the cold and have frost damage. Don't be so quick to remove the damaged part. Those drooping or mushy leaves can provide insulation against further cold damage. We had a crop of banana trees get hit hard during a really bad frost, hit low, stayed low ,repeatedly, for three days in a row.Those leaves just drooped down dead around the trunk. The heart of the trees survived and they recouperated 100%.Trimming off the damaged or dead parts exposes more of your plant to further damage. Here in south Orange county it's hard to get some folks to let things be.They want their yard cleaned up NOW. They think they can buy replacements. Clue- if the plant is dead or damaged in your yard, chances are they are the same in your local nursery. So be prepared, be patient, and remember, todays dead plant is tomorrow's compost, is food for the next crop. Wise woman says"Your garden and your library are never complete".

Poinsettias are part of the Euphorbia family. All these plants have a milky sap that can be mildly to very irritating. Points are generally mild,reactions vary by individual.The plant sold as"Gopher purge"is another euphorbia.Their sap is what drives off gophers who nibble it. Bunnies don't like euphorbias either. There are many common plants that are way more problematic.Of course, we all know oleander, it is so poisonous it can make you ill just handling it. Even deadly. People new to California often pick these sweet smelling, profuse bloomers. Bad, very bad. Also of some concern is our English garden favorite Digitalis, "Foxglove". Wash your hands people, plants & soils can contain all kinds of problems.I rarely wear gloves, being blessed with a resistance to most irritants. But I wash my hands often. As for the medicinal use for Poinsettias, I just read it in the past couple of weeks and will try to re-locate my source.
