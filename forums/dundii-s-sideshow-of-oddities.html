10 Cat Taylor March 01, 2010 at 02:51AM
The Wild Man of Borneo was another attraction in that sideshow. Me 'n' my pal Joe Paulino (teenagers) ran the Cocoanut Shie adjacent to the sideshow in that dark corner of the fair. Peg Long our ECHS Drama teacher put us up to it. The Cocoanut Shie worked like this: We hung a cocoanut in a noose and let players hurl other cocoanuts at the noosed cocoanut from about fifteen paces. We charged a nominal fee. We padded the wall behind and the floor beneath the noose, so that cocoanuts wouldn't break unnecessarily. When a hurling cocoanut hit the noosed cocoanut, the cocoanuts would explode and the hurler would win all the broken pieces. Wow. A primitive and exotic snack! Joe found his way into the sideshow, -subbing for the Wild Man of Borneo or whichever human oddity didn't show up on any given day. I spent all my spare time dancing at Fezziwigs and continued working at Fezziwigs for the next 6 or 7 years.

13 Susie Marceau February 27, 2010 at 06:47PM
That first Army st. Dickens Fair also had magic in unexpected places. While hiding out in the rooftop rafters one day with Mark Siebenthal and someone else, making a picturesque display of pantomime characters draped over wooden beams, I heard the strains of an Irish acordian wafting up from Mad Sals. It was not the familiar meloncholic deeply emotive music from Kevin Keegan, but had a quirky madness of sorts to it. I kipped off the beam and wandered , musically drawn to see who was playing. I peeked around the entrance to Mad Sals and none other than the legendary Joe Cooley looked up from his accordian and saw me. his eyes widened at the visage of my impish whiteface character. Later that weekend I had set up with my 'cello ( in the same mime costume)and was performing the first Irish piece I had learned, an ancient homage, The Battle of Augurin (sp?). This time I caught Joe Cooleys curiosity and as he approached he exclaimed in his Irish Brogue, "and so now you're playing the Battle of Augerin!"
