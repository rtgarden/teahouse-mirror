17 [126ln5dg00p4l] January 01, 2010 at 12:41AM
I was bus coordinator for a few years, and the faire bus started from Dundii's house on Seward (with that outline of a corpse painted on the sidewalk, remember?) I got to know him on the bus and during dinner at Buttonwillow ( I remember he made his own bush jacket out of scraps of ornate brocade the truckers gawked at) Dundii saw our commedia shows and he and I had several long conversations on the phone which eventually resulted in my writing and directing one of the PDA shows at Southern Faire; he was patient and charming and funny. I declined at first, but he was persistent and had faith. Once I got into it, I realized the show should have been conceived differently---those musicians were very hard to move around the stage! All the wanted to do was stand and play making for a static stage picture. I made mental notes for next time---then the split came and I didn't get a second chance. I wanted to do The Birds of Aristophanes! I know the faire show was better overall because of Dundii: he was one of those people you shouldn't allow to escape, and I know he had a great deal more to contribute.

Dundii's musical training was at the Cincinnati Conservatory, as an oboe player, so his musicianship was not faked. He adored It was a Lover and His Lass, by John Runge, a collection of songs from Shakespeare's plays set to tunes from the Fitzwilliam Harpsichord Manual. 29 Murray then, now aka MAX December 30, 2009 at 01:34AM
I have SO taken for granted that aspect of Dundii. I had the great good fortune to know him intimately for more than 20 years, live with him, watching him at work and at play, helping him sew on ruffles, research questions. When I met him, like you, I was a product of middle class upbringing. He was very patient with me, slowly enticing me to embrace my creativity and to understand that I AM SOMEBODY. He would ask my opinion about something, and then gently tease an answer out of my reluctance, like teasing the end out of the tangle of a knotted string. He would point me to a book, and then allow me to become an authority by guiding my developing understanding, embellishing my understanding with tidbits of thought. He taught me to GROK, before that book was written. September 29, 1935 was his birthday. We should make it a National Holiday. I miss him so much... The Shakespeare costume I wore was an example of the way he would work. He made it for me in order to uplift the standard of wardrobe at the Faire. He led by example, knowing that if he put out there something awesome, others would imitate it and by doing so, unleash their own creativity. He led by example and innuendo.
30 Murray then, now aka MAX December 29, 2009 at 01:32PM
thus setting the stage for the creation of the guild system, in that he demonstrated clearly that if you wanted something done you simply went and did it and just contributed it...he was such an ahead of his time artist he was actually doing what we do today in sharing culture and social media. As a child Dundii was part of a split world for me: on one side the dirt brown boring world of avocado colored refrigerators and ,mindless basket weaving of the suburbs: on the other side a vibrant world of flash and substance where people seemed to live in their bodies and even to still play hard in them even after they were older. He was even better than that for me he was a rare adult who showed that it might be even more fun to grow up and get to be an adult than to stay a kid forever. I barely made it into the show in time to just catch the tail of his coat. I knew he was flash though and like pure magic he went away so fast. Its often like that with real avant garde artists, like Bob also they tend to burn the candle at all ends, anywhere they can get a light.
31 Murray then, now aka MAX December 29, 2009 at 11:36AM
Dundii had decided that the Queen's procession needed dressing up. It was a shame, he thought, that the Ladies in waiting were walking in the dust while only HRH rode in a sedan chair. The original concept was that he would create the pageant for the Faire, and I was sent to SF to lay the proposition before Phyllis. She, however, would not even receive me, let alone listen to the idea. Not to be denied, Dundii then financed the entire project himself, and *rented* it to RPF by the season, enlisting the talents of a wide variety of musicians, whom he trained, playing music, which he selected and orchestrated, on instruments, which he commissioned, wearing costumes, which he designed and created, with the Ladies riding in sedan chairs, which he had made. He enlisted the best choreographers & dancers, Sergey Diaghilev couldn't have done it any better.
