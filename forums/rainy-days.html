I remember being in that sandbag brigade, but I think it was Frank Stoneh supervising. I could be wrong. I remember stacking sandbags and watching the river rise until it broke through, so all our work was for nothing. But we were laughing all the way. That was the soundtrack, whoops, hollers, and laughter.

18 Greg "Grego" Dana February 04, 2008 at 06:37AM
What really surprised me, after being so used to both Agoura and Blackpoint sites becoming unusable with just a little rain, was going to other Renn. Festivals (I don't like to call 'em Faires) and finding that they did NOT close for rain. In the mid '80s, I flew out Houston for Texas Renaissance Festival (TRF, also known as the King George Fair) to run a booth for Feather (that's a story and a half in itself - some other time). It would rain almost every weekend and it didn't really phase anyone. Not the boothies, not the customers, no one. The fair just kept going unless the rain got rather heavy. The amount of rain that would turn Agoura site to mud soup just drain away like water off a duck. People kept their goods out of the rain, but not, necessarily, themselves. A year or two later, I drove my 1972 VW bug (w/ a cart on top that looked like a coffin) to Colorado to run a cart selling Carol Williams jester dolls. The cart thing was not a good idea, 'cuz it rained there almost every weekend as well. And the mgmt. there was really not inclined to call a rainout, even when it was amply justified. The cart did not provide a waterproof sales space, so it was rather hard to sell jester dolls in the rain. For that and many other reasons, the venture was not a financial success. But back to rain ... unless TRF, when it rained at Colorado, the site did get quite muddy, and it was almost all on a slope, which made the mud that much more interesting. Also unlike TRF, the rain in Colorado was COLD, which made the rained-upon COLD. One saving grace was that in the camps surrounding the actual Festival site, campfires were allowed. I shared a campsite with Pagan and Gene and we had a nice firepit. Of course, when it was raining it was rather difficult to start a fire. Overall, I was happy to get back to California ... both times.

27 Greg "Grego" Dana February 03, 2008 at 07:55AM
No, DB was not there, most of the time anyway, during that rainout in the 80's. But I was, with several other mullettes who didn't have anyplace offsite to go (or that we cared to go). I remember how, if you started walking at Passhouse, you'd have a good couple inches of mud on the bottom of your shoes by the time you got to the Coffeehouse. We spent lot's of time sittin' in the coffeehouse (it was relatively dry), drinking coffee (or Green Death, dependin' on who you were), smoking cigarettes or whatever else there was to smoke (for those who did such at the time), and watchin' the rain. I remember one year, can't recall which one, when workshops rained out. All the participants were parked in the lot closest to the site ... the one you had to drive down to the earthen bridge to cross the creek, then back up. To get out, of course, you had to drive up to the racetrack from the earthen bridge. I remember this 'cuz I was in the parking lot w/ assorted other folk helping people get their old clunkers up that muddy road. Helpin' consisted mostly of pushing while the rear tires spun and splattered mud all over those who were pushing. I think I went to my father's condo in Liberty Canyon for a hot shower after that one. And I think the '77 rains were during my first year on crew. It rained prefaire then, too, and someone woke me up one morning so I could come down to help save the creek crossing below Passhouse (again, an earthen crossing). We filled and stacked sandbags in the pouring rain while Bruce Bagnoli supervised and passed around a flask of some type of hootch (wiskey? scotch?) to help us keep warm. Yup, that was fun. I'm purty sure I couldn't get my car off lot that time to get to my dad's place for a shower. Trekking in the mud and rain to the crew showers, just to get muddy and wet on the way back was kind of counterproductive.
