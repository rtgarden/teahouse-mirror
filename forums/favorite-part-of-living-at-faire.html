25 [360yq79w676f2] January 06, 2009 at 01:06AM
My first occasion for living at Faire was in '88, when we were prepping for Old San Francisco on the Pier. Michael Beard was in charge of Camping then and he stuck me and my tent on the Gray Barn Hill flats. Mine was the only tent there. I had the flimsy plastic pegs that came with my six-man (read: one-woman) tent, so there wasn't much purchase in the hardpan dirt. The wind came up that night, the tent pulled most of its pegs, and I ended up crawling out of the flattened, twisted heap next morning. Rope Joe then loaned me a sledge, so I managed to sink or break all the pegs. The wind came up again that night, etc. I don't remember if I got permission or forgiveness, but I moved my tent to a platform under a tree, right across from Crew showers and the Burlap dye vats. The platform was just big enough for my tent, my "kitchen" (milk crates, ice chest and camp stove) and a folding canvas rocking chair. After work and a shower, I sat on my front porch and watched the sun set behind the Mt. Tam range. I had the view all to myself for maybe two weeks, and I slept soundly every night. For whatever reasons, Michael waived my camping fees, so I got the million dollar view for free.

36 Greg "Grego" Dana January 16, 2008 at 07:45AM
I never lived on site more than a few weeks past closing, but between the end of Southern and the end of Northern, I lived on snobhill ... one season; Cardiac Hill (two or three seasons? can't remember for sure; in the "Dustbowl" one or two seasons (I can't remember, but that might be from all the dust). Later on, I just had a hooch behind the Coffeehouse. My favorite part of being on site was always just after Southern, getting to the Northern lot when relatively few other folk were around. The wet years were especially fun, since the front area (Ale 1 / Market Cross) was still a big pond. I also liked walking the fire roads and hiking the "back country" on the horse / game trails. As nice as the relative solitude was, the community that formed when more folks got on site and we all did what we did was magical too. I think I always aspired to winter over, but never managed to have (1) a job on a crew that justified staying; and (2) a trailer that I could actually survive the winter in. All the same, I thought of the Lot as home for years.
