One of these encounters took place on the Main Stage at the Queen's Show in 1973 where Dundii was magnificently portraying Mars in a little Allegory. Somehow I had been given the task of organizing a group of Little People who were "invited" by the Queen to attend. I rounded up a couple of kids, some more short people and an actual midget who was dressed to the nines in a court dress. At the last moment I recruited Peter Kapp to pipe us up to the stage for the entrance. His height also helped convey our littleness.We seated ourselves stage right. When Dundii made his grand entrance engulfing us with his great swirling red cloak, he realized that he had narrowly missed trampelling the Fairy Folk. I will never forget how (without dropping a beat from his lines) he conveyed to us with one look -an expression of genuine apology and deep love .

Richard Chase had a falling out with the faire over the Horn Dance. Now is probably the time for the Morris Historians to step to the front of the comment line, but I have read his letter to Ron, still in the company files, and he expresses grave disappointment in the Patterson's interest in making the dance more appealing to the general public (you have to admit, it's a very slow, somber, and some might say staid dance). Richard Chase had personally reconstructed the living dance from historical sources and would broach no discussion of variations to his set choreography and so He parted ways with Theme Events. This lesson has always tempered my thoughts about LHC and the old faire being true to history. Authenticity is indeed in the eye of the beholder and we sought to make it both accessible and authentic, but not slavishly so.

john... johnniZanni offline 8 Dundii & Inigo Jones Sat, September 8, 2007 - 7:25 PM in response to: Re: Dundii Dundii got interested in the masques presented at the court of James the I & VI, by Inigo Jones. Shakespeare did a send up of the courtly masques of the time in the tinkers play in "Midsummer Night's Dream. There followed a period of research (all Dundii's interests led to periods of research) in the history and origins of these Courtly entertainments. The Masques originated from the Mummers/Guizers plays... traditional celebrations of the vernal equinox. At the Agora Faires these were represented by the Morris Dancers of Richard (....?), the old man with the staff, goatee, remember him. First off, then Dundii and I went to see him and get resource material & references. The Guizers (pronounced, geezers) Plays were passed down orally, and they are still a living tradition in some places. Ginger Osburn used to say, "What happens on Hobby Horse Day is not grounds for divorce!" After grounding in this source material, Dundii then researched Inigo Jones himself, his production techniques, costumes, scripts, etc. All very thorough in his usual manner. Then he wrote a masque, the one with himself as Apollo. It was structured along the lines of P.D.Q. Bach's oratorio "The Seasonings." I recall there were songs for the four elements, Earth, Air, Fire, Water. Fire's song contained the words "Fire, Fire, Fire...." sung at the top of his lungs. This was first performed at Blackpoint, in the middle of a burn ban, and even the word "Fire" was forbidden. We had to say "Smoke" instead. Well, at the first performance of the Masque, that song brought the Guard crew running! Dundii was forced to tone it down, and the performer was forced to mime saying the words. This was just the beginning of his forays into the Masques. Then came Faust, and maybe others I don't know about. Dundii's musical heroes included Jules Hoffnung, Spike Jones, and P.D.Q. Bach. He only stole from the very best.

john... johnniZanni offline 8 Re: Dundii Mon, September 3, 2007 - 9:51 AM in response to: Re: Dundii What I recall of Dundii's early life... He was born on September 11, 1934. I assume it was in Galveston, though his mother's side of the family came from "Greenwood on the Yazoo," as he used to say. That's in Mississippi. An early memory he recalled was collecting rainwater for his Grandmother to use for shampooing. She would give him a penny a mason-jar full. When he was young, he was of average height, and plump. When he was I guess about 13, going through puberty, during one summer, he contractet Scarlet Fever and Rheumatic Fever at the same time, which kept him in bed for the summer. The combination of the two diseases and puberty kicked his pituitary into high gear. When he got out of bed, he had grown to 6'9' and was the tall endomorph we all know and love. It also suppressed his pubic hair. He was unable to grow a beard until he took Testosterone treatments sometime during the 70's. Good old Dr. Basil ...? (he was part of the Texas mafia, too. A friend through Gary Travis, he graduated from Baylor Medical School as an Osteopath, but California won't license Osteopaths, so he had to practice as a GP. He was Dundii's lifelong physician.) As I said, during WW II there were anti-german mobs protesting at his house. This led to psychological problems that he dealt with in analysis some time later. He said that during High School he was straight and sexually active with girls. He attended the Cincinatti Conservatory, playing the oboe. He gave it up, according to him because the double reed vibrating inside the head produces insanity. Whether that's true or not, I don't know; Dundii was convinced that it was so. This training is where his considerable ability in reading and orchestrating the music from the Fitzwilliam Harpsichord Manual for the PDA came from. An influence in that direction was John Runge's collection "It Was a Lover and His Lass," which is a setting of the songs from Shakespeare's plays to airs from the Fitzwilliam collection. I wish I could tell you more about his early life, but he didn't talk about it much. When he did, it was as "give and take" when he was getting me to tell him about my formative influences. One of the things he liked about me was my love of poetry, and efforts to write. When I first began living with him late in 1961, we lived in an old two-story house on Sunset by Western. The apartment was the second story of the house. At that time he had a number of cats, there were about 6 or 7 of them, as well as an apricot standard poodle "Caesar." One day he was talking about Dorothy Parker, and a poem of hers that he loved: "Pigeons on the grass, Alas!" He said he wished there were one about cats on the roof. This is because the cats were fond of sitting on the roof of the house. How they got there I will never know, it was a two story house. Cats never let you see them fly... I came up with "Cats on the roof, Aloof!" and Dundii loved it. It was as a result of that he and Patsy taught me how to smoke the Pipeweed. We had lived together for several months, and there was not a hint of it until then. When he was younger, like in High School, I'm thinking Dundii was a force to be reckoned with, in a way that he later outgrew. Vindictive, you did not want to cross him. One of the ways of revenge he told me about was to get an oyster shell (easy to come by in Galveston) and walk around the person's car dragging the shell along the paint. It's true, even when he was in his maturity, you didn't want to cross Dundii, but he would let you know right then, in no uncertain terms if you did. He outgrew the passive-aggressive way of dealing with things.

catt catt avery online 190 Re: Dundii Fri, August 31, 2007 - 7:55 PM in response to: Re: Dundii Dr Dee was in search of the Enochian language. essentially the written language of the angels. He felt that the symbols of astrology were remnants of that secret system of writing that was known to the ancients. Early on he was well knows as a mathematician as well as a succesful astrologer (no doubt sidereal). He was even imprisoned early on in life for having predicted the final triumph of Elizabeth I. Once Glorianna ascended the throne he was taken in as a fixture of court. he rode hard with Phillip Sydney and that bunch. His wizardry extended to navigation as well as most of all of what was then still thought of as "natural Magic". Somehow he seemed to also have a finger on the other type as well, and he had a mysterious and typically Welsh hand in the shaping of even the idea of the "British Empire". He had a steady heavy hand in the events at court during a very intense period. Eventually he met up with the strange and somewhat mysterious Edward Kelly. An alchemist and a scryer Kelly had the secret to the Enochian Language, and Dr Dee was certain that indeed he was having a direct conversation with angels, using Dr Dee as a sort of gathering rod. The combined effect of Kelly's copious writings and Dee's certainty helped to push Elizabethan efforts over the lip of the cup so to speak. The angels told Kelly to seek recognition further afeild. By the end of the eighties they were wandering in Europe looking for support from the Polish King, or some other monarch who could grasp the significance of the angelic transmissions. In the end there was a sort of bitter parting of the ways. The book I read had a more detailed and reasonable version than is often told. Eventually he returned to Mortlake where his instruments lay in ruin and his ibrary's were ransacked. Elizabeth set him up as warden of Christs College of Manchester. It was a difficult position to bear, with the onrush of protestant and puritanical leanings in the politics easily overlapping into the local opinion of him as a self-proclaimed Magus. Essentially Dr Dee felt that numbers themselves had magic, a god given magic that was deeply intertwined with their individual nature. Like most hermetics he felt that he each number corresponded to an angel and in turn a sort of set of characteristics. He was inspired by his understanding of the mystical nature of the given numbers to try and bring together the bloody divide between Protestant and Catholic, he even delved greatly into what could be understood at the time of Islamic scripture and philosophy around numbers and navigation and caballistic angeology. Essentially eventually he was treated in court with scorn. Once the deed was done, once the British Empire was invented, it had no place for the inventor. He was part of the world which he himself cracked open and stepped out of.

johnniZanni Re: Dundii Sat, August 25, 2007 - 4:57 PM in response to: Re: Dundii With your permission, I would like to hereby copy over some posts I made to other RPF sites here, so all the Dundii stuff can be under one thread. IN NO PARTICULAR ORDER======== As I can best recall, the first appearance of HRH QE 1 was at Agora, the fourth RPF, would that be 64 or 65? The event was an afternoon Main Stage Performance of "1603" by Mark Twain. Julie Meridith played HRH for the first time. I played Sir Francis Drake. The next year, I heard through Dundii that Carole La Fleur wanted me to play Shakespeare, and I needed to know a sonnet. At the time I had been hanging around SF, so I went to the City Lights Bookstore and memorized a sonnet. "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" And then hitchiked to Hollywood just in time to get my costume and audition for Carole. She approved the choice, and a couple of days later the Faire opened and the first Queen's Progress took place. Julie rode a horse, followed by The Firesign Theatre who were playing Lord Mayor, Lord High Sherrif, etc. Also, there were sundry SCA members from Berkeley who passed for "The Court." I followed the horse as groom, and pushed its rump next to the stage while the wrangler who led the horse kept its nose fixed. This was so HRH could dismount directly onto the stage. Later in the show, after proclamations, guild presentations, etc., I was introduced as a writer of sonnets as well as stablehand, Master William Shaxpur, did my bit, and that was it. This was wayyyy before PDA. The SCA group educated us in etiquette. But it took Dundii and PDA to bump it up a notch, and there was no rivalry between SCA and PDA that I know of. Mutual admiration mostly. I once offended an SCA member at a northern Faire, and was challenged to a duel. I had Therl for my second. We met behind the burlap curtain at the extreme end of the fair, just past David Teske's star map. As the challenged person, I should have gotten choice of weapons, but it was decreed that we would fight with rapier and dagger. I had done a little fencing with foil and saber in college, but that was new to me. Then we squared off, and they realized that I am left handed, and he didn't want to fight me like that, so I had to switch hands, with the rapier in the right and dagger in the left. As a result, my opponent got in the first few hits, but I got the hang of it and after that we were hitting each other about even. After a little while, he declared himself satisfied, and the duel was over. To this day I do not know what I did to offend him... One side effect was that Dutch Smith passed by during the duel and stopped to watch. A couple of days later it was his birthday, and a surprise party was held for him at night. The Guards wanted me to get drunk and disorderly so they could bring me before him for Judgement as a treat before they sprang the surprise on him. I had a great time "fighting" Bobcat and Woz, and then they presented me to Dutch... and he let me go!!! Therl later said any other actor would have been beat up and 86'd. I guess he liked the duel. It was the next year that I was asked to join the Guard crew, they wanted someone who knew the entertainers to guard backstage. That began my second career at the Faires. The PDA did play some gigs at Disneyland, but as a turn of the century Brass Band Ensemble, in parades and I think a Gazebo. I tried to get Dundii to put less energy into the Faires and go for more LA gigs, but he resisted. It was the main reason we parted company... Patricia Smyth nee McGurno was Dundii's "Sister." They had been "Beatniks" together even before I met them in 1960. They loved to dress up the same and go places and terrify people. She was an accomplished Astrologer (Siderial). Patsy had a long career as an extra and stand in. She was stand in for Tina Louise, etc. She was tall, slender, long black hair, and loved to laugh. She and Dundii kept each other entertained. When they were together, unless you could keep up, stay out of the way! On her applicaton for extra work in the space where it asked if she could drive, she answered, "All makes and models, Foreign and Domestic." For example... was at Dundii's memorial. I don't remember the year exactly. I knew Dundii from about 1972 when he rode the Faire bus (which stopped at his house on Seward in Hollywood); I later wrote and directed one of the PDA shows on main stage. The story was that Dundii had not been seen or heard from in several weeks, and somebody (I think George Alspaugh, the choreographer for the PDA) went to Dundii's house to check on him, only to find that he had been ill for several weeks and may have been trying to keep out of the public eye. This was in the early 80's when many people were getting sick. Eventually they got him to a hospital where he was diagnosed with pneumocystis, and he later died. Looking through his apartment later they found a typewritten parody of 'Kublai Khan' by Coleridge (it was a big poke at the Faire) which I had written and forgotten about---so that ended up being read at the memorial service by Luisa (Lois A. Plug, Dundii used to call her). The memorial was at an Anglican church in Los Feliz (in LA) and was crowded with Faire people---and also Dundii's sister, a very nice, genteel lady from Texas who I remember looking something like Lily Tomlin. Somebody who was up near the podium said that during all the speeches, he noticed a big black spider had lowered itself down from the ceiling and it just hung there awhile, as if listening. When the speeches were over, the spider ascended back toward heaven. Afterward we all talked, then several went out for a big Tex-Mex dinner in the neighborhood somewhere, telling Dundii stories. I remember Don Brown was with us, and Luisa, I don't remember who else. I know Richard Beard is on some of these Tribes, maybe he remembers more. Oh, Disney. Yes, Dundii did a lot of work for Disney. I remember one of his favorite costumes of all time was when he made some wardrobe for an elephant for Disney. He was a master cutter. A designer could show him a sketch of ANYTHING and Dundii could cut the cloth for it. He was provided with a team of sewers for these projects, mostly if not all ladies of hispanic persuasion. He called them "My Tacos." I never went to that shop, but got the impression it was a sort of Disney Subcontract Sweat Shop, although Dundii never exploited anyone who worked with or for him. Used, yes, but only in the very best way. When a Genius uses you, you find that you can do things you never would have imagined. I remember one night show especially, they were doing a rhythmic, repetitive piece which kept winding and winding, and progressing chords, and finally they came to a climax by changing rhythms and keys simultaineously that sent chills up my spine. The crowd went wild. It was a musical education for me. The only time I have ever seen anything remotely similar was at a YES concert at Hollywood Bowl. Dundii and Bob met at the Faire. No, the Queen's Progress was a fixture from the second or third faire on. Originally she rode on a horse. As Shakespeare, I first appeared as a groom, and part of my job was to push the horse's rear up next to the stage, while the wrangler who led the horse held its head. This was to allow HRH to dismount directly onto the stage. Then, later in the show, I was introduced as a lowly stablehand who also happened to write sonnets. I would perform one, she would receive me, and a star was born... By the way, did you know that the first group of Fairshire Officialdom (Lord Mayor, Lord High Sherriff, etc.) was played by the Firesign Theatre? Later, HRH began being carried in a sedan chair, with a bunch of SCA and Entertainers and Guild Members walking through the Faire to the Main Stage. Dundii dressed it up with elegant Ladies in sedan chairs, each accompanied by her Lord. But you knew that! I had gone on to other things just as the LHC was being created, so I never have even been there. Sadly, I know nothing of the PDA after 1976. But it sounds like the kind of thing Dundii would have done. Although I was never a PDA member, there was a certain bond there, and I did encourage Dundii to stop doing the Faire (he was becoming displeased with things, and when Dundii was displeased...), however it was not as a result of my urging that anything happened. Our last conversation on the subject ended with him agreeing to stop working the faires and promote the PDA in the LA arts scene. Then as he turned and walked up the hall, I heard him think, "I'll say yes, and do it anyway." I thought back at him, "If you do, I won't be here when you come back." And sadly, that's the way things fell out. That's one of the few moments in my life I wish I could recall... He did work the next Faire, and I did move out. How PDA came to be is an interesting story in itself. It starts with Dundii trying to shame the Faire into doing better wardrobe by making a stunning Shakespeare costume for me. Then he began making stellar wardrobe, first for himself, and selected friends. After several years of this, it was around 1971 or so if I remember correctly, he decided to present an offer to Phyllis. He would create an ensemble to dress up and authenticate the Queen's Progress and Main Stage Show. He would make the wardrobe, have period music instruments made, and orchestrate period music from the "Fitzwilliam Harpsichord Manual", all of which would be owned by the Faire. Of course, he would do all this for a fee. I was sent to SF to make the proposal to Phyllis. Phillys was having nothing to do with it. She would not see me. She would not speak to me on the telephone. She totally stonewalled me. Dundii was not to be denied, however. He raised the money to buy the cloth, and made the costumes. He raised the money and had the period instruments made. He assembled the membership, created the PDA, orchestrated the music (he had me writing out the sides!) and trained the musicians, Lords and Ladies. I think he did talk the Faire into providing sedan chairs for the ladies, I don't recall our having to make them. And then he rented the lot out to the Faire at a higher fee than he would have given it to them for. That is how the Faire came not to own that wardrobe, instruments, etc. I was never a member of the PDA myself, but was on the scene during its creation. It was a supreme act of will on the part of a polymath genius and his friends and admirers and students. Did you know Dundii was a graduate of the Cincinatti Conservatory of Music? He was a self taught designer, cutter and maker of wardrobe. I remember going with him to the LA County museum, and stalking the Renaissance section. Once when he was looking at an especially elaborate gown, he said, "There's a copy of my very next original." His motto was, "When you stand on the shoulders of Giants, you see farther." He also was fond of saying, "I only steal from the very best" In re: meeting Dundii. I was 20 years old, wet behind the ears, and attending Midwestern State College in Wichita Falls, TX, when a former student of the ballet school run by Frank & Irina Pal, one Joy Moffett came to town to make a film of one of their original ballets. I had served my Equity Apprenticeship at the Alley Theatre in Houston, and gone back to school for an acting degree, and was able to help them with the lighting of the project. As they were leaving to return to Hollywood, where she had offices at Paramount, Joy spoke to me those fateful words, "If you're ever in Hollywood, look me up!" That was near the end of the summer session, and it came to pass that three weeks later, I boarded a bus for Hollywood, and never looked back. When I got off the bus in downtown LA, I was met by Gary Coster, who had directed the ballet epic. There at the table in the cafeteria sat the strangest looking person I had ever encountered. Long face, pointed chin, widow's peak long black wavy hair with a slight green tinge. He stood up: unfolded, I should say, and rose to his full 6" 9" height (the tallest person I had ever encountered.) Later he told me that I looked like I was about to run back to the bus and head for Texas again. That I was totally intimidated, I will admit, 'twas true. I settled in as a roommate with Gary, and Dundii began calling to talk with me, (we were a Texas trio: Gary from Waco, me from Pasadena, Dundii from Galveston) and so we became friends. He was working for Logan Costume Company, and got me on helping during the Halloween rush. Gary convinced some ranchers to fund a movie (a western: Sundown at Devil's Flats) which we collaborated on writing. I was made Production Manager/Assistant Director, a job which I learned by doing. Dundii, naturally did the wardrobe. A good time was had by all. The picture was a Dog and never enjoyed release. Gary and I had a falling out, and Dundii invited me to become his housemate. I did so, and thereby, as the saying goes, hangs a tale...
