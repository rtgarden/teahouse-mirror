3 Murray then, now aka MAX March 04, 2008 at 02:33PM
this is the southern humboldt method: dig a pit the size of the garden you want, if possible two plus feet deep. We used to just make them the size of a truckbed. Fill with compost. From where you are I would find a horse (careful of green manure), rabbit or chicken farm. We also use a lot of leftover "soil less" mix. Like Luisa said you grow the soil, In part you want to be careful with too much ash because worms are the key. Let the worms do the work. Darwin wrote a great book on worms, they can work your ground up to 25 feet down, bringing up air and even water at times in the process. Look for those little piles they make, and also the leaves that have been skeletonized by their rasps at night. Then increase the colony! GIve them shade, often I have a lot of large potted plants out at the the edge of the garden which are also on the drip system, that give shade and shelter to the wild worms. I learned this trick observing Sioux Ashe the dancer and jewelers marvelous garden in the hollywood hills. The pots get special waterings also. Drip system, its easy. Use a cork-screw to punch the holes...the tools they sell suck. Get a nice timer. If youu are going to garden in california you had better set up a drip system and a timer and turn it on earlier in the year than you think you need to. We are all want to get traveling to see each other when the weather gets good so...make sure you see those flowers and eat those veg. suggested plants: wild strawberries pineapple sage (might do ok) impatients give that color carpet feeling in acidic shade. Look at what the local pro landscapers do in town around the banks and such on the shady side of the buildings in the redwoody type areas. They know how to do that "year round color" in your region.

Good Morning, Sandra: Your prayer of 'sweet undisturbed sleep' was answered last night. Many thanks! Feel good this morning, a rare and much enjoyed pleasure. I asked Mark, my beloved garden guru, and his answer to the 'deer resistant native plants' is to do several things first: 1) walk around your neighborhood and see what works for the neighbors. When you see something you like, then *look* hard at the micro-climate, mini-ecosystem that their yard is and compare that with your own. Sunnier? Shadier? More northern exposure than yours? (Or eastern, or whatever, you get the drift.) 2) Is there a nursery near you which hires a 'Master Gardener' who is familiar with your area, both in general and/or neighborhood specific? Ask questions of him or her. 3) Although we live near some very good nurseries, we actually consult and buy from an organic farming catalog called "Peaceful Valley Farm and Garden Supply." Yes, they do have a website and here is the URL: http://www.GrowOrganic.com We have had excellant results using their products over the years. As for the acidic, clay-like soil, well ... Mark is an engineer by training and so his answer isn't going to be simple or easy. I'll try and summarize it as best I can: 1) Try not to fight what you've got. Find out what the soil pH is before trying to change it. Also figure out if the plants you eventually choose will actually *like* what you've got already, soil-wise. 2) Clay: well, you do want to deal with that. Mark's first recommendation is to try and make the soil 'fluffy' so water and nutrients go down around the roots of the plants. Also plants like to sprout and grow in 'fluffy' not hard caked soil. In the Peaceful Valley catalog there is product listed you *might* use: 'CALCM+ Duel Purpose Gypsum' (catalog #F1152 (50 lb bag) @ $5.99 each (plus shipping) You can also find this on their website with the product description and suggested use. In part the catalog says "... used to moderate pH ... blah-blah-blah ... and loosen clay soils ..." Ok, sounds good so far. You'll have to read the rest online (pffffth!) (grin&wink) 3) More on the 'fluffy' soil idea; Mark also suggests finding from a local source some ultra-fine garden sand (intended for soil enhancement). The idea is to loosen up the clay and improve the water percolation. 4) Okay, other thing before digging and planting too much: when water 'perc's', where is it going to go? That means, right now, clay soil sheds water and so far everything has adapted to that. When you change to soil, the water will perc in down where the soil is now 'fluffy' but then it's going to hit rock, clay or something and run down ... to where? So the big thing is to think about DRAINAGE now and work around that. Some ideas: a) is your yard sloped? Might consider terracing and using drainage pipes (found at any decent hardware store; ask the gardening section and/or plumbing guys). b) is the water going to run off out of your yard. While you're watering in the summer, probably not. However in the rainy season a whole lot of precip is going to hit your newly changed environment and depending on how rain much falls at once, you could have a nasty 'gully washer' and/or lose the soil you so painstakingly enhanced. Also to consider the 'where will water go' question: oh yeah, where is your neighbor's house, anyway? Or garage, or swimming pool, or whatever. Good neighbor policy to find out in advance, since way too many folks don't have a sense of humor after a hard rain storm. Same thing with public roads, walking paths, etc. Water run off from your yard during a storm could be a costly 'boo-boo' and major embarrassment. *And* you may have lost all your spiffy fluffy soil, too. Nah ... you can avoid that. Just run a hose over your proposed planting spots and see where the water runs. Then figure out where you would 'rather' have it go. Whew! Long answer for a 'short summary.' Sorry for the bandwidth. BTW: Both Mark and I are SooOOOOOoooh *envious* of your redwood forest home! Can you post photos? I'd LOOOOVE to see your sanctuary. Hugs, Luisa, the Loquacious
