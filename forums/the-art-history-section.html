7 Eric Lethe January 29, 2009 at 11:39PM
Having finally "done" BurningMan this past September, I can see why some folks think its the "next" RenFaire...its not the permissiveness, nudity or pseudo-survivalism (how hard is it to survive in an air-conditioned Winnebago?) but the craft ethic I found resonant. Because we had a *killer* craft ethic. Where else would you find people who would seriously save their pennies for a hand-cast pewter goblet or hand-made knee-high moccasins, or real chain-mail head-dresses, or any of 100 other beautiful things? The fact that our crafts space was juried (meaning more people wanted to exhibit / sell than there was room for in most years, so they could afford to be picky) meant we got museum quality art out there...I attended an exhibit at SF MoMA a few years ago where they showcased objects made in that period, many by people we knew and loved. Hand-made, with skill and pride and love that shows through even 40 years later. So at BurningMan, although there is an unfortunately tendency to the disposable experience, there is this "art installation" ethic that involves a lot of wrought iron, a lot of fire...but if you look closely you see some really beautifully crafted pieces along with the MadMax schlock (especially amongst the Steam Punks, of course). I'm also reminded that when I first took my kid to Waldorf School, I immediately recognized it as tailor made for Faire parents...for the same reason...the Craft Ethic.

Ooh, post-modernism. The Ren Faire phenom as cultural pastiche, good theoretical framework, although I am so relieved I got out of college before that mind set really took hold in the liberal arts. OK, I could definitely argue that the way we understood and applied the concept of living history, taking bits here and there, and assembling it into an updated form so it would entertain, attract customers, and sell beer could be understood as fitting the genre of '70s - '80s pastiche from a post modernist perspective. I'm thinking of Queen's Bohemian Rhapsody in this context. Fair Port Convention is part of this and the musical diaspora that emerged from that kind of celtic/folk rock fusion into mainstream pop culture. I remember how popular Frank Zappa's (or his daughter Moon Unit) song "Valley Girl" was during the Faire of late '70s, both in that so many of us had grown up in the faceless industrial wastelands across the greater Inland Empire and could so relate to the satire, but that the humor and energy behind that kind of music was weirdly appropriate to the Faire experience and what we were trying to relate. In a similar vein, no accident either that the Firesign Theater could cut its chops on Faire stages and then move on to such classics as Ralph Spoil Sport Motors, or Happy Valley Condoms, If You Lived Here You'd Be Home By Now. There is huge high voltage cable connecting these on the surface separate expressions of the same cultural milieu. Repo Man also comes to mind, though it came out in the mid-80s.

Warhol: in school I studied him quite a bit. He was an early agitator for the happenings and the sort of large scale dance parties that we had as a secret component of our culture. When Warhol quit traditional fine art he did it in a big way. He made the first mylar baloons carefully by hand and filled them with helium, he then let them out the studio window in front of a shocked packed room of potential buyers and art critics. After that he went on the road with the velvet underground and revived the disco ball which was an older invention from the 20's. The reason I mostly mention Warhol here is that he had a lot of clever ways of catching peoples attention, and one intention, and one of them was books. He would make short runs of semi-handmade books and give most of them away to prospective galleries, collectors, museum directors, etc etc. Andy was early aware of the power of free. Once these collectors had some of his work in their hands they had a reason to make it become something of value. So creating the first roough runs of books at like 100-250 at a time, is sometimes a good idea to consider. Some of them can be sold or auctioned to help pay for the cost of producing a "real" book, some of them can be handed out as treats and favors to the same people who will likely cross promote the *book* when it is made real. Also there are specific aspects of faire that it would be neat to go into further depth into in later issues after people have already seen the first effort in one form or another. Once we have a sort of process in order we can condsider a more detailed account of say bellydance and tribal dance, the folk music scene, the faires as they spread out, the guilds...etc etc.
