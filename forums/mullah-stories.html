The Story of Bambi at Mullahs: I was working the early morning & evening shifts at the Coffee House serving breakfasts & dinners and also during the week when Faire was closed to the public. One day a friend of ours (Bobbi Enos) brought some deer meat, an entire hind leg. There was so much meat we served it all week long. I remember Jude coming up with names on the menu like, Bambi & Eggs, Bambi Tacos, Bambi Pasta, Bambi Burgers, Bambi Steak, and so on and so on till we finally got rid of it all. I think everyone was ready to kill us by the end of the week and didn't care if they ever saw venison again.

Those were a major influence on Don to call himself the Mullah, albeit he spelled it differently.

7 [24qnrwrde3u7f] January 16, 2010 at 06:00AM
I worked at the Mullah's for years, after hours, Friday nights and sometimes Monday mornings. I think it was 1983 the first year, or it might have been '82. In about '87 or '88 we followed the Faire for a couple years, living on lot and I worked during the week too. Sometimes the food was excellent, sometimes only passable, but there was always filling food. One of the mullahtos used to refer to some of the ubiquitous stews and soups that were less than successful as: "Boiled sea cow. It's not very good, but there's lots of it." One night we had one of these meals and the comment was oft-repeated. That night, after all had been cleaned up and everyone gone to bed ("Last call, we never close)," Barry and I drove out to the beach and filled up a garbage bag with sand and one with seaweed. We went back to the tea house and poured the sand in little piles in front of the tea house and draped seaweed artfully on the ground in front and a few pieces on the counter. In the morning the tea house opened up and found it had been visited by the legendary sea cow. Until now, we never said a word, just let people wonder.

14 [29p7u2atfcx87] June 12, 2009 at 12:00AM
I first met Don at the Dickens fair in 1972. At that time I think he was calling his Tea House "The Persian Tea Room". It was my first year performing with Bal Anat at the Faire and he invited us to perform in his Tea Room at Dickens. I even have a video of that show. Darioush had been playing Santour for us and got into a disagreement with Jamilia and ended up quitting. There was a replacement of musicians and I remember Sol was playing Saz, someone new on Santour and Mark Bell and Don played drums. He was a gracious host to all of us and it wasn't until the following year at Southern Faire that I saw him again. I remember him warming his way into our hearts back then. I danced at the Faires from 72-75 with Bal Anat and then Babaganoush. Took leave for a while and returned in the mid 80's to work with The Bells, other guest musicians & Sage. That year (85?) was when I first became a Mullette. Don let me build my hooch in back of the Coffee House so that it would be convenient for shows and work the booth. I'll never forget this first hooch. I remember trying to dye my fabric a kind of brown/burgandy color and for some reason it came out a flaming pink! Don dubbed it "Mish's Pink Palace". Every time he'd walk by, he'd just give me that look of OMG. Wouldn't say anything, one always knew just by a glance if he disapproved of something. But then he'd shake his head, through his arms in the air, look up at the sky, shrug, and laugh. I loved dancing in all the shows but couldn't wait for them to end and start my shift end of day in the Coffee House and then again in the mornings. I loved all the interaction working behind the booth and meeting such an incredible group of customers and ending up becoming part of this wonderful family of co workers and new friends. Some for life. I think my days at Faire are some of the most memorable and happiest days of my life. As many others, I didn't care if I got paid it was something I felt just to be able to be a part of Don's generosity. Working for him, I admired the whole set up and scene he was trying to create and am so grateful to have been allowed to be a part of it. It was such an honor the first time he handed me the Sooty Bag. I knew then that I had his trust as a friend and worker. It amazed me too how loving he was to everyone. He gave so much away. People had tabs that we kept written down in a little box that somehow sometimes would magically disappear. A few time I watched him tearing up someones card that couldn't pay him back due to hard times. He knew at sometime they'd be there for him. And just look at all the love and support being paid in honoring his memory. He was and is still loved by so many. We will all miss him so. Some of us are better people today, myself included,for just having the priviledge of being a friend of Don Brown. Rest in Peace Dear Friend. I love you and miss you but will always remember you. Thank You for being you and who you were to me.

The Mullah was a river to his people. We were Don Brown's people. The river was his welcomingness, his beingthereness, his creating a space-to-gather-in-ness. My second booth was right across the road from his tea house. Mad musicians playing all night, crazy flamenco that I didn't then appreciate but now treasure. He was always needing paper for another sign. I kept making it for him. He gave me free chai for life. I don't think he knew how young I was or how long he was going to keep doing the faire. I remember Don always making sure that the musicians could get in through the gate. If he could do it through proper channels he would and if he had to ask a favor for that tab box he would do that too. All the worlds top traditional musicians knew that if they could just get to the lot, they could get together and play and share out what they had discovered in the past year. This was a tremendous engine in the yearly procession of talented musicians and artists that graced our days and nights at faire. Mullah always kept track of our merry band and made sure we could get together and share the special times, at faire, at mill, at lark, at harbin and many other places along they way.
