In another thread it was said that fairy rings made by witches may have protected Caravansary Canyon. If so, the fairies never lacked a stubborn little warrior in their corner. Some of Theryl's ashes are there. He would drop by my camp during lunch break. Sweltering days they were, and I would offer him one of what I was drinking, fizzy spring water. "Too rich for my blood" he'd answer. Bob Mcgowen had another way of saying that: "Costs more than beer and doesn't DO anything!" So out from the cooler would come a Guinness, which met with no such excuses. Knowing how little I drank, he could easily have surmised that he was the main reason I kept stocked. It was at one of those times that he taught me the difference between blarney and bullshit. Blarney, he maintained, is never malicious. He was so full of it you could squeeze any part of him and it would be like milking a ripe cow. You you'd drink deep, and get some of it on ya, but you could never believe any of it. On the other hand, doubt him at some peril. He had lots of facts alright, but created and lived within an aura in which, as in poetry, mere fact was trumped by something, well, as BobT would say, "else". One scroungy day at downtown Wizzin's me and him were cruising the thrift shop. I guess maybe we weren't in our Sunday best, probably a comb shy of impeccably groomed. So as I browsed the shirts this kindly church lady type suddenly steps up to me and hands me a couple of dollar bills, wishes me well, and blesses me. She was so danged sweet, so sincere about wanting to help out, and I coincidentally had my wits about me at the moment, so I just accepted and thanked with a matching sincerity. Watching from a discreet distance, Theryl was busting at the seams over me having been pegged as a waif in need of charity. Not only did I hear about it in blazing technicolor all the way home, but from then on it was a standard end of the month routine. As thoughts turned towards empty cupboards and skinny wallets he would lean towards me and say "Hey Greggy, let's us go down to Sally's Army and work the marks." On a fine Blackpoint day, just before launching a Bones Band set, I filled in the gang regarding the route dejour, mentioning a couple of special hauntings that would occur along the way. Later that day, in a private moment after the dust had cleared, he leaned into me in that way he does and said, almost in a whisper, "Greggy, you were directing!" Now ya gotta know that I, the crowned King Cag, headed up an ad hoc band that famously never rehearsed. So the intimacy of Theryl's tone implied that if this anomaly he had noticed ever went public reputations would be at risk. The secret would be safe with him. He was wrong, of course. I was just passing along some information.

13 Murray then, now aka MAX December 01, 2007 at 10:41PM
Another thing that Therl and I got into with the Houndsditch Thespians was producing parades. It started at the RPFS when the Punch and Judy Show needed a way to gather an audience for their morning show. With the help of Carolie, a bunch of us got dressed up in random fantasies, along with the two guys who were doing the show. We had a big bass drum, various noisemakers, etc. Then we would gather at the main gate and form a procession. Of course, a crowd would follow, and we ended the parade at the Punch & Judy booth/theatre. We re-created this for the second Dickens Faire, where the show was renamed Punch & Toby. My dog Glorfindel was in that parade, too, wearing a ruffled collar. He had an entertainer's pass, in the name of Glorfindel Dogge. The gate guard insisted on stamping his paw... He was the only dog in Fairshire history to have a gate pass. Another parade we created was for the RPFN. In the afternoon the merchants in the upper arm were complaining that no customers were finding their way up there. Therl, Ginger and I put together a Druidic Procession. We had a big gong suspended from a pole, borrowed from Willie Brewer, conch shells and assorted strange noisemakers. Dressed in black robes, we would proceed from Main Gate up through the faire, to the end of the upper arm. There we would burn incense and mutter cryptic nonsense charms. When we were through we would disappear behind the burlap, leaving the bewildered crowd that had followed us scratching their heads, and free to find their way back down the lane and to the mercy of the booth hawkers. There was a time we did a variant of that Procession at RPFS. There was a booth selling little whistle type thingies that you held in your mouth and it made a whirring whistle. These things drove Carolie Tarble up the wall. She talked to the booth people several times, trying to dissuade them from selling the things. They were'n period, and were commercially produced, not a craft. All to no avail. Finally, Carollie gathered together a group of people together backstage, dressed us up in black, hooded robes. Sporting the aforementioned gong and conch shells, and carrying a brazier of hot coals, we wound our way to the booth in question. Without a word, we surrounded the booth, and with the mournful music, ceremonially Carolie dropped some of the whistle thingies onto the burning coals, where they went up in smoke. The rest of us turned around and filed away, leaving Carolie to say the only words spoken; "This is your final warning," she said. Then she turned around and walked away. The next morning the booth was struck, the vendors had gone, and they never returned to Fairshire.

There was a night show at the northern faire one year, and my partner in the Houndsditch Thespians, Therl O’Ryan, said we had to have an act, but it had to be one no one at the Faire had ever seen before; everyone at the Faire had seen all of everyone’s acts and wanted something completely different. It was after morning progress, and I was in my Guard uniform backstage when I got the news. I thought what the heck, I’ll write about Hyde Street Fair. So, I picked a tune, "Paddy West’s House" and started out. After a While, Woz comes up to me & sez “I hear you’re writing a song about the Hyde St. etc. There better be something in there about a six inch fireboat hose connection.” “‘Scuse me, sez I. What exactly is that?” He explained that the S.F. fire department had required Service & Supply to provide a six inch pipe with a connection that a fireboat could connect to in case there was a fire at the fair. They spent all of their budget on that, and that’s why there were no drinking fountains on that site (answering a complaint of mine at the time!) I also have to note, that there is one verse of this which Therl refused to sing with me at the time, out of better judgement; He was probably right, but I went ahead anyway. I’ll let you guess which verse that was. Therl was always quoting some arcane source. I sometimes suspected he was just making it up, and creating an authority for his ramblings. As I have grown older, some of these sources have fallen into my hands, and I have had to revise my opinion of his knowledge. He was probably one of the most widely-read people I have ever known. He showed me his poetry once. A ring binder volume of typewritten pages of the most ecstatic writing I have ever seen. I wonder what happened to it? When Therl was living in Willie Brewer's basement in Martinez, I stayed there for a time. He had just broken up with Ginger Osburn, and was in the pits. Willie had laid in a stock of Bass, Steam Beer, Mead, etc. left over from a Dickens Faire. It was enough to have lasted him the winter. Therl had gone through it in several weeks. My job was to pull him out of the depression. We would do things like go up to the cemetary, which is on a hillside on the west side of Martinez, sit by some grave and do poetry improvisations. Therl thought of them as "Battle of the Poets." He would start a theme, then I would respond, he would reposte and we would go on for hours that way. There was a bay laurel tree there, and when we were finished, we made laurel crowns and presented them to each other.

The Hells Mouth was made originally for a production of an old English Easter Pageant at RPFS. The play was about the Passion, Death and Ressurection. Instead of having pageant wagons, the crowd followed the main player (J.C. Himself) from place to place about the Faire, where the scenes would be presented. The players in that scene would then join the procession. It all wound up at Main Stage where the Hells Mouth waited. In this scene, Christ was to call forth Satan and Beelzebub and open the gates of Hell, bringing forth the Saints and Sages who had died before him, and transporting them with him to heaven. I played Satan and Therl WAS Beelzebub. The entire show was concieved and dressed as a Masque. We all wore paper-machie masks of our characters, and nicely costumed by Carolie Tarble (of whom not enough is being said). My costume was all flowing black gauze. One day Therl talked me into smoking a long, crooked cigar as part of the act. He was always improvising, as you are well aware. When you went onstage with him, be ready to go with the flow. It was your job to bring the scene back to it's path, after whatever diversion he wanted to throw out. So... I got one of those long matches for lighting fireplaces, and went out onstage with the cigar, swiped the match across the stage floor, and set my costume on fire!!! We managed to get it snuffed before I went up in flames, but after that I told Therl... "No more cigars!" Another intersting story about the Hell Mouth took place at 3rd Dickens Faire. We were building it in a warehouse on some old Navy property. I was the electirician, and didn't have a helper. Working loooooong hours. One night abut 2am I was looking for somewhere to crash. It was cold and damp, and I finally crawled into the Hell Mouth which, was being stored by the construction office, and bedded down for the night. Next day I was talking to Woz, who had been on guard that night. He told me that the Hell Mouth was Haunted! He had heard loud moaning and weird noises coming from it the night before. Did I mention that I snore?

1970 Unspecified warehouses near the Embarcadero near Union & Battery (first faire - 3 weeks) (I think it was the Old Icehouse, now gone). 5:00 to 11:00 pm, Tuesday through Friday, noon to 11 pm Saturday & Sunday. The article mentions Jay Paul and James Kahlo, as well as Consortium Antiquum. It also says that Ron & Phyllis "have done a total of 12 Faires, here and in Los Angeles" Yikes! Such babies. My favorite quote from the Pattersons references both fairs, Renn & Dickens: "Our favorite periods and both times when there were queens." Well, yes, and....LOL. I WAS AN ENTERTAINER AT THIS FIRST DICKENS FAIRE. THE WAREHOUSE WAS BETWEEN THE VORPAL GALLERY AND BLUE PETER'S SHIPYARD. IT WAS ACTUALLY SEVERAL WAREHOUSES AND YOU HAD TO GO OUTSIDE AND AROUND THE CORNER TO GET FROM ONE TO THE OTHER. IT WAS THE FIRST TIME THE ENTERTAINMENT DEPARTMENT COMISSIONED PLAYS. SEVERAL OF US RPF REGULARS WERE DETAILED TO DO A VICTORIAN MELODRAMA, "BLACKEYED SUSAN." THERE WAS A CAROLING PARADE WHICH KEPT GETTING INTERRUPTED BY THERL O'RYAN AS SCROOGE, I REMEMBER A SKITTLES BOOTH, OF COURSE MAD SAL'S WHERE WE PERFORMED "BLACKEYED SUSAN". I PLAYED DOGGRASS, THE VILLAIN; JUDY ? PLAYED SUSAN, GINGER OSBURNE PLAYED HER FRIEND, (FORGOT THE CHARACTER'S NAME), AND THERL LATER JOINED THE CAST WHEN ONE OF THE PLAYERS DROPPED OUT. BLACKEYED SUSAN WAS AN INTERSTING PIECE, BECAUSE WE WERE ALL MORE OR LESS METHOD ACTORS, BUT WE ADOPTED THE DEL SARTE TECHNIQUES IN VOGUE DURING THE VICTORIAN ERA. WE BROKE THE PLAY DOWN INTO BEATS USING METHOD, THEN CHOREOGRAPHED IT WITH DEL SARTE TECHNIQUES. IT WAS AS TIGHTLY BLOCKED AS ANY BALLET YOU WILL EVER SEE. J. PAUL MOORE WAS THE MASTER OF CEREMONIES, AS I RECALL, LEADING THE PARADES AND DOING CLOSE-UP SLEIGHT OF HAND. HE WAS GOOD! I ALSO DID SIDEWALK CHALK DRAWINGS. THERE WAS A SWAP BOOTH THAT STARTED OUT WITH JUST A FEW CHEAP TRINKETS, AND BY THE TIME THE FAIR WAS OVER, HE HAD A LARGE INVENTORY OF JEWELRY AND GOLD ITEMS. THE GUY WAS AMAZING... 1971 Wharfside, next to the Cannery, Fisherman's Wharf. As I recall, this was when the Cannery was still in construction (more or less) and the fair was in the base of warren of brick buildings. Cold as hell. I was 13 or so and went with my sister in costume. Thursday and Friday, 5-11, Saturday noon to 11, Sunday noon to 8:00 WE GOT THIS EMPTY GROUND FLOOR OF A WAREHOUSE TO BUILD THE FAIRE IN. IT WAS STREET LEVEL ON THE EAST, BUT BELOW STREET LEVEL ON THE WEST. THERE WAS A SECOND STORY BUT IT WAS USED FOR STORAGE AND ADMINISTRATION, SIGN SHOP, ETC. I WAS THE ELECTRICIAN. SINCE THE CANNERY WAS STILL UNDER CONSTRUCTION, THE TRANSFORMER AND POWER POLE FOR OUR SITE WAS TAKEN OUT THE WEEK BEFORE WE WERE TO BEGIN CONSTRUCTION. THE PLAN WAS THAT WE WOULD PLUG INTO THE POWER AT THE HYDE STREET PIER RANGER STATION JUST ADJACENT IN THE SAME BUILDING. THEY HAD A SYSTEM THAT REQUIRED US ORDERING A SPECIAL PART FROM SQUARE D IN CINCINNATI OR SOME SUCH PLACE. THERL WAS THE PURCHASING AGENT, AND HE KEPT ME POSTED ON THE STATUS OF THE PART (THERE WAS ONLY ONE IN EXISTENCE, AND WE HAD BOUGHT IT!) AS IT WOUND ITS WAY THROUGH THU UPS LABYRINTH. THIS WAS WAY BEFORE THE WEB... IN THE MEAN TIME, WE RAN THE CONSTRUCTION OFF OF A GENERATOR OUTSIDE ON THE STREET, WHICH I HAD TO KEEP FILLED WITH FUEL. THE PART KEPT DISAPPEARING EN ROUTE. THERL HAD BOOKED ME A SEAT ON A QUANTAS FLIGHT TO AUSTRALIA, IN CASE THE PART NEVER ARRIVED. WHEN THINGS WERE GOIN WELL, I'D WALK UP TO HIM AND SAY, "CANCEL THAT FLIGHT!' THEN NEXT DAY THE PART WOULD ARRIVE IN SF, BUT GET LOST IN TRANSIT. I'D RUN UP TO THERL AND SAY, "DON'T CANCEL THAT FLIGHT!" IN THE MEAN TIME I HAD TO WIRE THE ENTIRE SITE WITHOUT BEING ABLE TO TEST ANY OF THE CIRCUITS. FINALLY THE PART ARRIVED, AND WE HAD A BIG CEREMONY WITH ALL THE SS CREW IN ATTENDANCE. I HAD WIRED INTO THE CIRCUIT A FLASHPOT THAT EXPLODED WITH SPARKS AND FIRE WHEN WE THREW THE SWITCH. BIG CHEERS. UP AND RUNNING. THERL, GINGER AND I HAD FORMED A COMPANY, "THE HOUNDSDITCH THESPIANS," AND WE PRODUCED A SERIES OF THREE MELODRAMAS BASED ON THE CHARACTERS FROM "BLACKEYED SUSAN' WE HAD PLAYED THE PREVIOUS YEAR. IT WAS COMMEDIA IMPROVISATORE STYLE THEATRE, WHICH IS TO SAY THAT THE CHARACTERS AND PLOTS WERE SPECIFIED, BUT THE SCRIPT AND ACTION WERE IMPROVISED EACH TIME. THE SAME PLAY, BUT A DIFFERENT SHOW EVERY TIME. THERE WAS ALSO MISS LYDIA PINKHAM ON THE PIANO. SHE WAS A POLYMATH MUSIC/MATH GENIUS FROM BERKELEY. SHE IMPROVISED MUSIC TO GO WITH OUR PLAYS, AND ACCOMPANIED ALL THE OTHER SINGERS AND PERFORMERS AT MAD SAL'S. THE HOUNDSDITCH THESPIANS ALSO DID A TAKE-OFF OF JEKYLL AND HYDE CALLED, "SEEKING MR. HYDE." J. PAUL MOORE AND THE CHAMBER COMPANY DID A MEDICINE SHOW. BILLY SCUDDER DID SONG AND DANCE NUMBERS. JUDY WAS JUDY... THERE WAS A FORMER MOUSKETEER IN THERE SOMEWHERE, DENNIS DAY. WE ALSO DID "PICKWICK CLUB," WITH EVERYBODY DOING THEIR SOLO BITS, SORT OF MODERATED MUSIC HALL STYLE. THERE WAS A 'PUNCH AND TOBY' PARADE IN WHICH MY DOG "GLORFINDEL" PARTICIPATED, WEARING A RUFFLED COLLAR. HE WAS THE ONLY DOG EVER TO GET AN ENTERTAINERS PASS AT THE FAIRE, TO MY KNOWLEDGE. IT SAID "GLORFINDEL DOGGE." THE SS CREW TOOK TO THROWING TOMATOES AT ME DURING OUR PERFORMANCES, AND I WOULD RETURN FIRE WITHOUT INTERRUPTING THE ACTION. ALL A PART OF THE ACT. HOWEVER, THEY THREW AT THE MOUSKETEER, WHO COMPLAINED TO CAROL, AND THE WORD CAME DOWN... NO MORE TOMATOES. EVEN SS WAS AFRAID OF CAROL. THERE WERE NO MORE TOMATOES... UNTIL... THE LAST DAY OF THE FAIRE I WALKED INTO MAD SAL'S TO FIND A CRYSTAL PUNCH BOWL FULL OF TOMATOES, AND ON TOP WAS ONE WITH MY STAGE NAME ON IT... MR. JUSTIN CASE. THERL ALLOWED AS HOW WE WERE IN TROUBLE. I AGREED. WE MADE A PLAN, AND IN MY NEXT POST I WILL TELL YOU THE TALE OF "THE BATTLE OF MAD SAL'S."
