Roll Call of the Ages.... topic posted Fri, December 1, 2006 - 3:58 PM by Noel on tribe I'm floating this out there. Let me know what y'all think. Given that history seems to belong to those who write it down (whether or not it's true seems, at times, moot), I thought it might interesting in this Alumni Tribe to post a roll call of sorts. Rydell's post in my Phil Robledo thread got me thinking seriously about this. There seem to be lists out there. As we all get older, and busier, and fuzzier, it would be nice to not have to reinvent the wheel each time we think about taking the old memory chariot out for spin down memory lane (like that extended mixed metaphor?) I started keeping a "list" about 10 years ago when I realized I was forgetting more than I was remembering. It's worked (to an extent), but it might be nice to have something for this particular group of lunatics to refer back to. St. George uber alles. No contention, no grandstanding, no flaming. I'm seeing a year by year, by Northern and Southern, sign in sheet. Each of us has very strong (and oftern different) recollections of what was what, but we all might be able to agree to disagree about who was where and when. The numbers won't be that large (there were never that many of us Georgians and Proto Georgians) and I'd like to keep it as apolitical as possible. None of that "they did this to me, or not, or whatever." If anyone's interested in this this, post here. This is will be a collaborative effort ("Do you remember that guy with the glasses....") and a communal one. We can build it ourselves and just sort marvel at it as it develops. Recall lost friends. Honor them. As Dido sang, "Remember me, but not my fate!" Gail and I (and Donna Moran if she's still here and Bruce Roberts) are uniquely situated to list the Oldest of the Old Guard (1974 or 5 to 1979). After 1979, I'm out of the picture. I'm not sure what the drop dead date would be (it gets political there), but I'd leave that open for discussion with those who were there. I wasn't. I've been thinking about this for quite awhile. It first raised it's head when I started reading about punk rock and found that my history had been co-opted by folks who I don't even remember being there. It came up again for me when my honey Brandon was watching the Cockette movie. He turned to me and said "Those people weren't really Cockettes--they were just hangers on!" I just had to laugh. Everyone gets so exclusive, but I think there's plenty of room for everybody, just so long as we keep it civil. So, what do you think? As I say, I'll take a crack at Northern 1975 (I'll need a lot of help here) and then 1976, 1977, 1978, and to some extent 1979. Be rigourous with yourselves. Check it what you think you know. I've been shocked at something I thought I was certain about turning out to be completely, totally wrong. It's very humbling. Noel Gieleghem Earl of Lennox (1976-1979) I'm going to keep soldiering on here for a little bit, although I suspect this may be classed as a "vanity" thread on my part. In other words, yes, I know this is all about me and my skewed perceptions, but I'm making my obsession public as perhaps my navel-gazing might be of interest to others for hysterical purposes. Everytime I read "official" Faire history (I just made the mistake of reading some of Phyllis/Paul cross-posting and it seemed a tad self-serving on everyone's part), it's usually so biased that it makes me gnash my teeth. By list-making publically (and collaboratively--thank you for your excellent input, Gail), it makes filling in the canvas a bit easier and, I hope, paints a truer picture. All of this doesn't really mean squat diddly to anyone else but me, but I find it a fascinating exercise in memory and rememberance, sort of public hypnotherapy or regression. Again, I kick myself for not keeping my journals and not having more pictures!!!!!!!! I think I have Fall, 1976 worked out sufficiently well that I can "work" it as a matrix for 1975 (Gail, you'll have to help me here as Spring, 1975 was the era of the not-quite-hostile take over by Patrick, et al., of the Court after his initial test flight of the Earl of Oxford at Northern, 1974. I know he deus ex machinaed his way into Southern, aided and abetted by Peg, did his turn on the stage, and then vanished into the mists of time.) That period (1974-75) is truly the Ur Court (a branch of Duffi's dance show - wish I had more of that history, but I was a fraidy-cat 16 year old at that time and much too terrified and awestruck to ask any questions). 70 PatriciaBlanco May 15, 2008 at 06:24PM
Ooooh, good question, Morgan. IIRC Theme Events got a liquor license fairly early (sometime in the 1960's, before I ever started working Faires). However, also during the late 1960's (or possibly early 1970's), there was the infamous Mrs. Walter Brenden fiasco, where Ms. B (I *think* a resident of Lake Malibu, just down the road on Cornell a short way) objected to County Officials about the 'drunken and drugged' crowds emerging from the Faire. This complaint led to an equally infamous video tape (or actual film footage) of a rather young female who *looked* totally out-of-it while at Southern Faire, and also *looked* like she was both under-age *and* was holding a suspicious bottle that suggested she as a minor who had been 'served' alcohol. That footage then became the justification to revoke the liquor license, and just about cost Theme Events the possibility of being shut down entirely. Fortunately, no one could *prove* anything about the image, the girl's *actual* reason for "wild" behavior or even how it was that she had that bottle in her hand in the first place. Theories abounded that the entire scene was a set-up, and/or that the girl was handed the bottle *by* the folks shooting the footage. Anyway, that *did* cost TE the right to sell liquor for several years, seriously impacting Faire income, and it wasn't until RPFS'74 (the third Faire I ever participated at) that Theme Events got a *partial* liquor license again. The partial, or restricted, license meant that each Ale Stand had to have a visible and physical barrier around it in which adults could purchase *and* consume alcohol beverages. Guards were posted at the exit of each 'Ale Garden' (defined by a large circle of hay bales) to make sure that absolutely NO beverages were consumed outside of the legal boundries of the license. I *think* that meant that Ale stands had actually two working sides, ie, for non-alcoholic beverages, one could go to an 'outside of circle' side, purchase whatever, pay, and just walk to where-ever to consume it. However, I'm not sure that system was all that obvious to a lot of customers, so I don't know how that may have affected over-all sales. The other drawback was, if one did indeed purchase a beer, or whatever, inside the legal 'ale garden' then one was 'trapped' until drink was consumed. So if one was a participant on a schedule, one could find oneself having to 'knock back' a beverage faster than what would be prudent, and then ... ak-BOING! Instant 'fuzzy head.' Same thing for ale customers. Either they were away from the shows and/or they just got drunker faster. Of course this doesn't answer your question at all, does it? Any old time beverage folks on the teahouse list? Yoo-Hoo? Any help you can give us here?
