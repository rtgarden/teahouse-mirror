Just a few quick notes. Haven't made cowbow shirts in years, however have been doing a bunch of fiddly "close" sewing recently (ruff making). Re yokes (and any sort of applique), baste the be-jezus out of what your doing, and press/iron as well. Some folks use a U-Hoo glue stick to keep the stuff in place as it washes out. And slooooooow down when you're sewing. If you deal with your maching stitching as a type of augmented hand sewing (by this I mean stitch-by-stitch-by-stitch, using the handwheel where necessary), you'll be a lot happier with the end result. When you start to get frantic, just remember that using a machine this way will still be faster (and perhaps more durable) than hand sewing, just not as fast as the pedal-to-the-metal sewing we're all used to. And don't do yokes (or collars) after you're tired or at the end of the project. You will always screw them up. Start them fresh, when you're not tired or cranky. And if they start screwing up, stop, take a break, and return to it. In shirt construction, you get a much more professional look if you use thinner thread (100% cotton embroidery, etc.). Also, shirtmaking is a good time to drag out and practice those weird feet (edge-stitching, lap seaming, roll hemming). I use a Bernina 830 and the all of the odd feet really come in handy. Also, look into a proper top-stitching needle (it has a large eye; buy Schmetz), and change your needle frequently for perfect stitching. You can also double-thread your top needle for a good effect. Re collars (and cuffs, plackets, and yokes, for that matter), try to locate a copy of David Coffin's book (or books). Google him. There's stuff on YouTube as well. He's pretty amazing, and has all sorts of non-intuitive tricks regarding collar fitting and turn/ease of cloth, etc. Sort of the tailoring approach to flat construction. I used his tricks when making Dickens Fair stand-up collars and was very pleased. Finally, if you can find someone who's done production/factory sewing, pick their brains. That world is full of tricks which I've never seen published (making "sandwiches," etc.) The criteria we bring to bear in evaluating shirts is lodged in the ready-to-wear/production style of construction, not it hand or fine sewing. Hand-sewn or homemade shirts always tend to look lumpy or "thick" (it's hard to explain why), because we tend to compare them to production-made shirts which use all the tricks. Hope this helps.

I've been using the Folkwear pattern for 5 Western Shirts($20.00-ish for pattern, though I could perhaps trace the pieces for you, there are a lot of them) Folkwear is nice because it has all sizes from S through XL printed on heavy paper. It only has women's size adjustments on the simpler shirts. The one I like to use also has decorative cut-outs on cuffs that are appliqued on. Some of the patterns are including up to XXXL, but not this one yet. It is spelled YOKE, by the way...the pattern has a stylized bull head & horn cut out for the yoke, that is finished with bias piping enclosing the raw edge. I've made up my own cut-outs(flying saucer, rocket ship, moon and star, a guitar...etc) The trick is to have a simple design that won't be obscured when the piping is sewn on. I usually sew it on by hand because it is easier to control when you have curving edges. Instead of trying to use an unbroken strip of bias(hard to turn a sharp corner because of the bulk of fabric)I use two or more. I cut my own bias from metallic lame, but since it frays, I dilute Fray-check with water and paint the edges with enough of a light coating that it keeps the edge from fraying away, but does not add globules of pesky glue. I fold it over cotton yarn and baste raw edges together, then line up the raw edges with the raw edges of the cut out. When it is appliqued to the shirt, the raw parts are tucked under and topstitched close to seam of piping and cut out. You can embroider over anything that shows, or add a layer of applique or...It does take a bit of delicate wrangling...www.folkwear.com, I think...also check out fringed cowgirl jacket pattern and cowgirl skirt.
