Funny story, back in the mid 70's we invited Uncle Dick to a party at the Narrow Byway (our nickname for a house on Arrow Hwy. in Upland Calif.). Among those who lived there were Doug Burger, Leesann, and Brian Bailey. We had some wicked good parties there. So, there's Uncle Dick sitting there with us. With many sidelong glances at each other we lit a joint and sent it around the circle, watching to see Uncle Dick's reaction. "What's this!" he exclaimed, and we all giggled and kibitzed and told him to smoke it. He made a show of "trying" to toke it. We were all laughing. He just kept hold of it, then got up and walked out of the room with it. It took a moment, but one of us finally exclaimed "That old man is bogarting the joint!". Yeah, he was a wily one. Turned the tables on us. Nope, couldn't put one over on him!

12 PatriciaBlanco November 13, 2009 at 03:49PM
I do so like the honesty of talking about all aspects of those passed. It keeps them human, and every part of them made them who they were. I love and respect him. And he was certainly opportunistic. The man had a hustle. But he had something to offer, something he seemed compelled to share. He HAD to pass on what he had,in the way he knew how, and that was the oral tradition. He also told me that for tradition to survive it had to change, because that is our human nature. The challenge was to keep the essence intact. On certain dance steps he was VERY exact. Some things had to be done exactly right. I believe those were the essentials he was trying to keep pure. It was hard to try to rein the exuberance of we youthful dancers. We'd put in spins, throw each other gleefully across the room, only to get stomped down hard by him. Not always, he didn't want to discourage us too much. But, yes, he was a mean old man. I know that he was often very hard on me. With today's eyes I can suppose that it was because much was expected of me...(cleansing breath)... It is not a method of teaching that builds confidence. Yet, learn I did. Some part of me was imprinted with a knowledge of certain things that must be done a certain way. His mark is on me, and I have tried to pass it on. It doesn't belong to me. But I have certainly been a channel. Recently I offered to share Ashkem Richard sword dance, as he taught it, with Red Rose Morris (as it exists today). After walking away 18 years ago, it is one of two dances I actually yearn to dance again. Some dancers replied YES!, yet there are those who tell me that the Askem Richard they dance is they one their research (into a written form) shows. Sigh. The oral tradition vs. the written. As soon as I saw that I withdrew into silence. This is not my battle. I only know what I know. I can only share what I learned. This dance was one Uncle Dick was VERY specific about. It's imprinted. It's a little sad, but I must accept that the one dance I really want to do again, I may never have the chance to again. But I passed it. I think, perhaps, I can understand Richard Chase as I never have before.

15 Greg "Grego" Dana November 13, 2009 at 11:29AM
Lest we stuff poor old "Uncle Dick" into a suit way too tight for even his skinny country butt, or forget the other sides of the tradition thing, here comes a reality check. If you surf out on a google Chase search you start finding bunches of praise, but also some mud slinging. Some academics, who of course argue endlessly among themselves, also sometimes act as though it were open season on those not of their ilk. They write off non-academic Chase as everything from a hack to a charlatan. This, generally in reference to his most famous work, the Jack Tales collections. Example: " The academy has long dismissed Chase, and rightly..." Example: "Chase's versions were 'less emblematic of the narrators he claimed to represent and more a reflection of himself.' Chase, a shameless self-promoter, had a well-developed talent for appropriation, as he subsequently came to assume "ownership" of the tales he published-one might almost say, of the genre itself-becoming not merely a collector but a performer. " From an academic point of view this would be serious condemnation. Then again, from a folky story teller's perspective, this might be exactly what you're supposed to do. On stage. Then there's off stage. There were some pretty groovy Claremont locals who avoided him like plague. While I attended a hip alternative "free school" there, the teachers would batten down the hatches at the very scent of his approach. He seemed to be as welcome as a cop at a high school beer bash. This was a quirky reaction I didn't understand at the time, and failed to ask about. Dorothy provided some clues. Also notable in the Claremont folk scene were Charles Chase and his wife Dorothy, the couple that founded of the venerable Claremont Folk Music Center. In a chat I had with Dorothy one day, mid '90s maybe, she was going on about the dark side of certain folk scene people. The man who called himself "Uncle Dick", said she, had been actually kicked out of his Appalachian community (maybe burned out, I can't remember clearly), for being a racist queer pedophile. She said that when he came to Claremont he used to claim to be related to her and her husband, which she insisted he wasn't. Painted him as a fibbing opportunist. Her words nearly knocked me sideways. Might as well call Santa Clause a serial killer. She went on to expose the stinky laundry of other SoCal folk icons. Maybe she was in a mood. Maybe to what ever extent our dance master and others were guilty of various picadillos, she too was guilty of tall gossip, if not more. Somebody I know that used to do instrument repair work for them eventually left with ill feelings, and told tales that tweaked my images of them. My world took a hard, kinky twist when images of people I admired so much were tarnished. Could the whole positive vibe enlightened folk revival image be a smiley mask over a pimply, very human face? Anyone who knew Wood Guthry would certainly say so. The movie "Song Catcher", while shining a revealing light on the problems of academia vs folk culture, takes you right to that pimply face. It shows the living, beating heart of Appalachian folk tradition pumping lifeblood into hands that sometimes do the devil's work as well as picking a mean banjo. Old world prejudice and hard ass attitudes are the skeletons in our closet that mirror bound feet in China and mutilated genitals in kinky fundamentalist Islam. The chorus probably sang real pretty songs in the churches of old America and Europe on the days they burned "witches" at the stake. Thankfully, lots of the dark side of tradition is history. But too much of it just got swept under the rug, and lurks in our customs, language, and culture. I guess I'm sounding off because there's such a tendency to idolize The Old Man. He gets a token humanization among us via "curmudgeon" type references, which may be letting him off kinda easy. I've been guilty of that, and of idolizing the Folk Music Center Chases too. Whatever liberties Chase may have taken, we can at least credit him with having been a huge inspiration to a whole bunch of us. We make take the same liberties he did by embellishing tales about him recreationally. But by way of thanks for his gifts, let's carry on here in our histories of him without idolizing, soft focusing, cheapening his humanity. Wicked John wouldn't be fun or interesting if he weren't wicked. To whatever extent there was wickedness in Chase, let's celebrate it as part of the legend of The Old Man.

17 Murray then, now aka MAX November 10, 2009 at 09:28PM
When Dundii got interested in bringing a group to the Faire, at first he was thinking about a Guisers play. So, we went out to visit Richard, and pick his brain about the men's ceremony traditions. Richard was very helpful, referred us to some resources, but was adamant that the REAL traditions are handed down by word of mouth. He offered to incorporate our effort into his traditional company. That wasn't what Dundii was into, however. I did write a "Fairshire Guisers Play," and showed it to Richard. He gave me some very kindly critique, considering it was make-believe to his REALITY. Dundii then started studying the Masques presented at the court of James the I & VI by Inigo Jones. His take was that these were derived from the Guisers tradition, and presented a framework more to his liking, being formal, elaborate, and scripted. The rest is history. From those researches came the PDA. I went another direction, and directed scenes from Shakespeare based in the Guiser's tradition, nobably the tinker's play from "Midsummer Night's Dream," on the Wagon Stage. But it all began with sitting at the feet of the Master, Richard Chase!
