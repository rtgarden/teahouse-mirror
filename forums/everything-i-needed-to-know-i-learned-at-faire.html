For several years I was the "Communications Coordinator" (better know as the Walkie Talkie Guy) for the California Traditional Music Society (CTMS) Summer Solstice Music Festival. The last year was 2 years back due to difficulty with the site. One of my jobs was teaching Radio Etiquette -- not to Security types, but to (mostly aging) folkies. I created a radio procedures manual, a very abbreviated "10-code" list, and even taught them to say "Carbon Condition." The Festival was such a tame event (no booze, biggest gate was prolly 4,000 for two days). I think I did that job for 4 or 5 years. Before that, I was the "Gate B" (i.e., staff and vendor gate) Coordinator. Where I also basically used what LHC taught me, and amazed many people by knowing what to do in various situations.

17 Greg "Grego" Dana February 04, 2009 at 07:03AM
I learned some important conversation skills from using the old CB walkie talkies: (1) LISTEN before you talk and wait for an appropriate amount of silence before you open your mouth; (2) Only interrupt if what you have to say is vitally important, and more important that what anyone else is saying at the moment; (3) Clearly identify who you are and who you want to talk to and make sure they're listening before going off at the mouth ("Security Base, this is Tree Person, do you copy?"); (4) Only one person should talk at a time; (5) You can't talk and listen at the same time; (6) It's okay to talk in code sometimes, but if you overdo it, no one will understand; (7) Speak clearly and in as few words as possible; (8) Sometimes you need to relay information from one person to another. Pay attention and repeat as closely as possible what the other person said; (9) When you're done talking, let the other folks know you're done and then shut up! ("Sequoia Clear")

I think it was Rollie who taught me how to walk the length of the Northern Faire site without a flashlight. It was basically a requirement. On Nite Security, flashlights were for reading passes and for shining in other people's eyes to keep them off balance (but not very often).
