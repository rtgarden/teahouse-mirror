14 Greg "Grego" Dana March 08, 2009 at 02:17PM
Ah, the barn of many incarnations. My first northern the teahouse was there. Our country dancers needed musos, and at a spool table sat a couple learning the tunes on recorders and fiddle. Ricker and Rita. History conniving to be made. The next year there was an 11th hour scramble to shlep livestock-related stuff into improvised storage areas to make floor space for the Balckpoint Music Fest. Piano on forklift. Elizabeth Cotton on stage. Redwing's girlfriend Daphne falling for Cathal of the Boys of the Loch, and later bugging off to Edinburgh to marry him. Later, as a carpenter, I was in on the long, adventurous transformation of that cinderella shed to a pretty princess of a theater. The sordid details of that project would best be thought of as having seeped into the bog with the sweat and second hand beer, but the results were the stuff of real fairey tales. Along with the night shows, dances, parties, et al, there were at least three shows to which we actually invited the public. (odd idea, but there you go...) The Music Fest I'm counting as one, even though there were a variety of events. Then there was a grand westward-ho themed production, Richard Beard designed, Will Wood etc, as part of the Old Cal show. What was the name of that show? And the fems-on-the-prairie musical, "Quilters". Kate Hefflin directed, Karan Hanson sang and acted beautifully with an otherwise non-faire-folk cast of women, and the orchestra was a piano guy served up with side orders of me and Ricker for folky flavor. Thus was germinated the oh-so-aromatic duet of Coot and Cag. Thus were made to be things that could barely have been dreamed.

Red Barn Memories -- there are so many! One that particularly stands out was after one of the Hedonist's Picnics up at the End of the World, with the requisite Grand Marnier injected strawberries and the Dill-Dough -- several of us climbed up the ladder and onto the roof of the Red Barn. One of the men taught me the fundamentals of dancing the Tango up on the slanted roof. Many of the folks were in a bit of an "altered state", and one of the women stopped breathing every time the Tango brought us closer to the edge than she was comfortable with. A grand time was had by all! All of a sudden, someone shouted out the alarm that Rick Bowman (Security) was driving by. Everyone FROZE and pretended to be invisible. Rick's car screeched to a halt, backed up, and then took a different road. After a collective letting out of our held breath, we all started laughing and continued on with our revels...

36 [2c9hwt6yphj6c] March 12, 2008 at 06:12AM
A couple of scenes vividly crackle my memory synapses. Both from when the Red Barn was just a dirt floor barn. The first time Dickens was at the Cow Palace (I've always loved that name) around the early '70's or so, Wayout Willie and I and some other burlappers were charged with making and installing decorations. Some of these included pine wreaths - boas actually. Very labour intensive. Pine boughs were cut and brought to the barn on the "beer van". We'd trim them, then wire 'em together so we had these conifer snakes that were about 3 to 4 inches fat and anywhere from 4 to 10 feet long. The joke was that we were making giant Thai sticks. We set up a tank with flame retardent and ropes and pulleys. Dipped 'em, hung 'em out to dry then trucked 'em to the site and did the instalations. Many hands for many days and for all that they were good for just the one show. Man, nobody could spend the deco-budget like Mom (Phyllis) bless her heart. Then there was the Spires/Brown Fracas. Some of it I recall viividly. A curious thing is that neither Will nor Don seem to have any recollection....hmmmm? Anyhow, the coffee house was first exiled to the barn sometime around '74 or so? It served as the participants commissary both during and after hours. I don't recall the public coming out to the barn at all then, but it had a small stage in the northwest corner all the same - for music, dance and of course the occasional poker game. So it was agreed that Don would do the coffee house stuff and Will would cook omlettes. One morning, I believe it was during set-up, I was sitting at the table on the little stage noodling on my guitar or rolling a smoke or something and our boys were in the coffee house; there may have been others in the barn but certainly not many. I become aware that there is a disagreement coming from inside the coffee house/kitchen. Voices are raised, then re-raised and a full blown argument is taking place. Somebody shoves somebody else then gets shoved back, then again once or twice more and the next thing I know Don and Will are rollin' and a tumblin' across the floor - I kid you not - coffeehouse rat as my witness. I jump up to go and try to break it up and my memory gets hazy about there -but I'll tell you this: no one was about to throw the first blow so no blows were thrown, but boy, those boys were trey-pissed at each other for a few days. It looked like the partnership was dissolved before it fully took off but somehow it survived that season and the Red Barn became ours for a while. P.S. - Don and Will (as many of you know) still make some of the finest coffee and omlettes available.
