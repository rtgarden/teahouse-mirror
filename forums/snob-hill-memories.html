Sunbathing on Main Stage in December: Not really a story so much as a description of one of the many âluxuriesâ we Lot Rats enjoyed in the off season. The Ben Johnson Stage at Blackpoint was back in a sheltered vale that protected it from the often cold wind that blew in midwinter making it a pretty nice place to hang out. In addition, it was painted a deep heat absorbing brown that soaked up the sunlight making it a WARM place to hang out. Now, timing was everything in this. Because of the relatively steep walls of the vale the sun did not reach the stage until about 9:30 or 10:00 and was behind the hills again by 3:00. So, for the best of winter sunbathing, one would arrive around 11:00 and leave by 3:00. But, for those four hours, one could often find a naked hippy or three basking in indolent luxury while the rest of Marin worked for a living. Good times, indeed!

Joelâs shoe tree and his quest for my corduroy slippers. Joel Ficklin was my neighbor on Snob Hill for three or four years and remains one of the best neighbors I have ever had. But, and those who know Joel will back me up on this, he was, and is, a bit eccentric. For the most part, his eccentricity was harmless and frankly fun to watch. One of my favorites was Joelâs Shoe Tree. There was a large oak tree just below his trailer and one day I noticed a pair of shoes dangling from a branch well up in the canopy. I shrugged and went to the privy. Over the next several days, more and more shoes made their way into the tree until there were perhaps twenty pairs swaying in the afternoon breeze. I saw him in the yard one day chucking yet another pair of old shoes into the tree. I walked over and asked him if was making a shoe tree. He smiled, pleased that I had gotten his visual pun. Then, eyes sparkling with mischief, he stared at my feet. âAnd I need those,â he said. I was wearing a pair of oh so suburban, Ozzie Nelson style, brown corduroy house slippers that I used as camp shoes. I told him that he would have first rights when I retired them. He hounded me almost weekly for nearly a year until I finally got a new pair of slippers. I took them to him and he gleefully tied them together with a piece of cord. Then, with the intense focus of an artist who had finally acquired exactly the right pigment for that perfect accent, he walked around the tree looking up into the canopy until he spotted what he was looking for. It took him three tries to get them just where he wanted them but seemed very pleased with the results. His clear delight was easily worth the price of the slippers. Eventually the tree boasted about thirty pairs of shoes in a wide assortment of sizes and styles and remains one of my favorite visual puns. The last time I was on the hill, just after we had cleared the site, there remained a single pair of weather beaten shoes still dangling in the old oak. I like to think they are still there.
