Reading through these postings, what came to me was to keep the 'methodology' of living history in mind as we work through this material. The historical mining is fun for us, analyzing the larger context and impact is worth doing because it was significant and not well documented in an academic sense. But as we keep pulling out memorabilia from old shoe boxes, I'm eager to keep in mind the continuing ripple effect, and ongoing cultural impact this Faire has today and going forward. It took several decades for the fruits of the 'velvet revolution' to tear down the Berlin Wall. There is the prospect for a secondary cultural impact here, that may be coming, from the seeds that were planted and the diaspora that was spawned.

8 Murray then, now aka MAX January 29, 2009 at 01:49PM
Eric I am hot on trail of any faire materials that may have been created by Sally Schneider. She was the mastermind of some of the design work, brochures, maps and whatnot for two influential stints. She disappeared rather suddenly under very suspicious circumstances (in 1983) just two weeks before she was supposed to move in with me in So Cal. We were very close, and her techniques and ideas as a conceptual artist have lingered with me and greatly influenced my own work. One of the things I would like to do is sort of celebrate her work somehow, and put her in the canon for the artwork that she did and the logo movement that she was a part of. She and Bob Thomas were in major cahoots as far as a lot of that signage and iconography was concerned, he even took over for her literally after she disappeared. Personally I have always suspected foul play regarding Sally connected with that den of wolves we had among us. regardless of her demise, or even if by some great fortune she is still alive waiting for a regime change, her work needs to be celebrated and understood. Part of what she would talk to me about at hours for length, was how to use maps as the base for creating a super saturated environment that has a series of stories within it which the potential participant is intrigued to seek and discover. She was creating a gameboard for the participant to literally walk into, the idea is that they would use this tool to travel there again and again in their imaginations before they would ever even start making their costume or heading down Cornel road.It was a special way of priming a huge participatory audience for a sense of tone, involvement, and place all at once. It was much much more than a map. kinda like a modern video/console game huh?
