Yikes! The text is HUGE! Oh well, at least we can read it, eh? However, this isn't the way we made the panniers for our summer stock production of "School for Scandal" way back in the seventies. Think more like a baby's bonnet, only with boning (we used the cheap but strong plastic in fabric ribbon boning). Using the idea of 'bread basket' try to imagine this: A regular, large, loaf of french bread. Cut off one end about 4-6 inches long. You should have now a cylinder with a round top (the cut, flat end is on the bottom). Now cut that cylinder in half, lengthwise, from the curvy top to the flat bottom. You now have two halves, curving out away from the straight vertical cut. Now imagine creating two fabric and boning 'baskets' that would resemble those two halves of cut french bread. We used solid fabric to hold the boning (much like the outer crust of the cut bread), and also used a flat piece of fabric where the 'inside cut' is on your bread analogy. This held up very well, though I don't think our panniers were collapsible. Then again, it was an outdoor ampitheater. Anyhow, I'll try and find better illustrations in other books. BTW: did you notice in the first paragraph, the author mentions that an earlier mentioned dress had the supports (ie, panniers) *inside* the skirt fabric itself? Can't find the exact figure referred to, however. Hey ho. BTW(2): the book is "History of Costume - from the ancient Egyptians to the Twentieth Century" by Blanche Payne (drawings by Elizabeth Curtis). Harper & Row, New York, 1965 (no ISBN).

Greetings Buffy: If I recall correctly from making similar 18th century costumes back in the 1970's for stage, the trick was to make two small panniers for either side (as opposed to one big hoop skirt with ties to make it wide and narrow, if you take my meaning). The panniers (which meant 'breadbaskets' originally, but you already knew that, of course), would actually be sewn flat, with smaller length of 'boning' at the top, and gradually the strips of boning would get longer towards the bottom. Then the entire thing was folded over from the middle and attached to a five to seven inch 'inside' piece that would be flat against the lady's hip, and the boning flaring out in a semi-circular half-bee-hive-sort of shape. Boning was put across the very top seam (going out from hip curving to outer side of 'basket') to reinforce the upward curve against the downward weight of the skirt fabric(s). Ummmm ... too much text, not enough illustration. Will look thru a couple of books nearby to see if there is a pattern I can scan and post here. Ciao for now. Luisa
