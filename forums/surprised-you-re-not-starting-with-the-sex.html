6 Murray then, now aka MAX April 13, 2010 at 03:12PM
I just picked up on this thread, so my comment may be a little late, but all these estimates about attendance are mostly correct in one way or another. The slowest gate at Faires (north and south) was almost always on the Monday of the run (Memorial Day/Labor Day), which rarely exceeded 8000 paid admissions. Other days that drew over 20,000 were considered good ones. But there were many days when attendance at Southern was over 30,000, at which point the Highway Patrol would put out traffic warnings to the local media, because the 101 freeway had become so backed up. Regarding the number of participants, everyone is essentially correct again. During my 2 decades at Faire, the number of performers (from the lowly to the exalted) probably maxed out at somewhat more than 2000 on any given weekend. And then there were also all the boothies, and their seemingly endless number of daily workers, some working for the entire run, and others who would "help out" only for a day or two now and then. I was once asked by an unknowing staffer at a staff meeting why the site seemed so crowded on Faire days before the public gates had even opened. I informed him that by opening time, the number of various particicpants on site (counting all the crew folks too) with gate passes or on the daily worker lists was sometimes in excess of 4000. All in all, the total could occasionally indeed approach close to 40 thou over the course of a very busy day and counting everyone on the lot, public and otherwise.

15 Greig Fors October 03, 2009 at 04:36PM
Eloquently put, Sequoia, When I started working security, there was a lot of animosity towards security. There were three reasons that I joined the "brotherhood of the tunic". 1) I was tired of wearing pounds of wool. 2) I wanted to change the ugly perception of security, and 3) since, as a booth owner, I had to be at faire and I figured that working for the company, they could pay me to be there (and it was the coolest job at faire). This was in '86. The idea of protecting our own against the tide of customers, was already in my mind, and as it turns out was still a priority with security. Access control played a big part at that point in time and continued to be a priority. When I started working security, there were a number of other people that joined the team at the same time, who also wanted to change the the face of security as well. Most notably, Eric Worth and a little later on, Sarah. Together, they brought a great deal of levelheadedness and humor to the job. Tearing participants a new asshole began to disappear and a more courteous security staff began to emerge. But, the idea of protecting our own was still there and at least for me, the customer was the second class citizen. We treated the customer like royalty, but secretly spoke ill of them when their backs were turned. It was us against them, I guess. And, yes, everybody at faire watched out for everybody else, security did not have a monopoly on that, for sure. But what security did have, was the authority, and that helped a great deal in putting teeth into the final decisions of what happened to the bad guy.
16 Greg "Grego" Dana September 08, 2009 at 05:00AM
When I was on Security, it was both spoken and unspoken that a good part of our job was keeping Faire Family safe from the outside world. That was both during Faire hours, after hours, and during the week. It meant watching out for pervs bagging on teeny-booper fairebrats ... but not (at the time) stopping the teens from experimenting and doing their thing, as long as all parties were consenting (no, we didn't always get that right). It meant keeping the private areas private, and occasionally meant keeping some of our own (performers, boothies, crew, or staff) from making complete arses of themselves in the public areas. Sometimes it meant making a few people feel very UNwelcome - usually those we perceived to be thieves, pervs, or otherwise a danger to our community and our home. It meant that, during faire hours, we allowed and encouraged the public to have a good time, but when (a few) got out of hand, it was our job to be the buffer and to "shepard" troublemakers out of our world (okay, sometimes they tripped a few times on the way out). And it meant keeping our playground private during the week. Did we always do it right? Hell no! Did the vast majority of us do it from the right place, and with the goal of protecting our unique Family? I submit we did, and that we did a damn good job of it considering everything. Our failings and failures have been discussed elsewhere on this group, and sometimes wiser heads have advised that we NOT discuss them. A few of those failures will haunt me (and I s'pect many other Security alumni) for the rest of our days. Security certainly was not the only thing keeping our family safe - we ALL did that. But those of us who wore that tunic did do our part, I believe, in creating a safe place for all of us to play - and for some of us to hide. When the job of Security became more about "Access Control" and less about watching out for our own, I found other work at the Faire.

22 Greg "Grego" Dana August 29, 2009 at 09:36PM
While I don't think "mental asylum" fits, it was an "asylum" as in the sense of "haven" or safe place for many of us. Especially for those of us who lived on site, whether for a month, a season, or years. The family at Faire let us be who we might not have been able to be in the larger world (at least, not w/o getting arrested or into other serious trouble). I remember when I was on front gate during the week (Scale House @ Black Point, "Cross Roads" @ Agoura). If the sheriff came on site, we couldn't necessarily stop them, but what we could do was delay them and, via walkie talkie, let everyone else know he was there. That was one of the more important parts of that job, along with keeping the outside world out of our world for that part of the week. While the "suspending" (or ignoring) of some of the rules of the "outside world" occasionally did result in grief, which has been amply covered elsewhere here ... or wisely left alone, it also allowed many of us to play, to work, to live, and to grow. To have relationships that the wider world would have frowned up, to discover parts of ourselves that we suppressed "out there" ... to do what much of the rest of society said NO to. And to put on a Hell of a Show, which would NOT have been the same w/o the "safe asylum" for those of us who were "out of phase" with the larger world, and has NOT been the same since what is left of Faire has changed hands and become more of a "family show" and (much) less of an "asylum" for misfits and wild peoples. Some of us WERE broken or "strange" ... but we were safe in our community (most of the time) and were given a safe space to be who we wanted to.
