8 Greg "Grego" Dana March 20, 2009 at 03:50PM
Headed south from Novato with Ruth, I was going to introduce her to Santa Cruz for dinner. Parked just off the mall near Laurel st. and took off walking. Lovely, old time feel to all those un-reinforced brick buildings downtown. Lots of stylin razzle dazzle behind big display windows. I had just bought a so-cool spider puppet in a groovy shop, and we continued up the mall when the velvet hammer vibe started building. It came on pretty quick, with a roar, like being surrounded by locomotives. Big windows began to ripple, wave, and explode. I pulled Ruth to where I could pin her back to a tree and cover her body with mine. Heh. Might sound like a fine position to be in, but thoughts of the whole town, maybe the whole coast going surfing, all those old predictions of Cali sliding away were flashing through my quaking head as the glue that holds everything together slipped under the strain. Dust filled the air, visibility a matter of yards, so there was a roaring invisible world that shook like a lot bomb with shattered shocks, and nothing to do but ride the sucker out. When things got suddenly quiet it took a moment for my mind to clear (street hippie truckin by going "wo, where can I get some more of that shit"), and a few more moments for the air to clear. Everybody dazed. Scoop of ice cream on the asphalt. Looking up the road in the direction we had been walking I could only see about 30 paces. A pile of bricks that had been a building blocked the view beyond that. If I hadn't stopped for a moment to buy that spider... We had soup and sandwiches at a church emergency set up, with lots of "know how to show a girl a good time" talk, and spent the night at chez Paul Thomas, sans electricity, amid chunks of fallen wall plaster. Next morning a transistor radio had rumors of a possible southbound escape route. Back road through Watsonville might be open, anybody's guess on where the next gas would be available. Well, shit, we had just finished the ?th annual last ever RPFN, and just survived a disaster movie, so we were no strangers to risky adventures. We bid farewell to those who had helped us, left the town to fend and cope for itself, and shamelessly rolled off towards the next chapter.
9 Eric Lethe March 02, 2009 at 06:01PM
I was in Oakland, on the 16th floor of the Ordway Building where I was working for a law firm. My office didn't have a window, but I wasn't in my office...I unwisely ran to the window so I could watch whether Adi's preschool (which had been built in 1907) was coming down. I grew up in Cali, have been through *lots* of earthquakes and they seldom scare me. Of course I knew I wasn't supposed to be that near a window...but...just had to make sure Adi's building was standing. Anyway, the Ordway is built on rollers, so it sort of smoothly tilts like a pendulum in a quake. Because I was looking that direction, I happened to see the Cypress Freeway crumble. It was far off in the distance, but I could see plumes of dust rising and thought "...Oh dear, that can't be a good thing...". Then the shaking stopped and I gathered up my crew and my traumatized kid and put them all in my truck (six guys in the back) and we drove to my house in Richmond because none of them could get home to the City. They all wore my nightgowns that night. We ordered pizza and watched the news. The next day I went back to the building and found every book in the law library on the floor, and all the files as well (the downside of pendulum action). Took about a week to sort out.
10 [1m2dkokp1oiys] March 02, 2009 at 04:42PM
I was on vacation from my weekday job, and had been on the lot since the previous Friday afternoon. Butch and I were supposed to go to Santa Cruz that day to visit one of my friends, but decided to postpone the trip until Wednesday or Thursday. I went into San Francisco to take care of personal business including leaving a message for my friend saying our plans had changed. I had returned to the lot at about 4:30 and was in Butchs pop-up trailer in front of the Red Barn when the quake hit. I was debating whether it was wise to remain inside, but as I looked out the door and saw Graywater rocking side to side under the water stand I decided to stay put. As I listened to the coverage on the radio and learned of the increasing damage I realized just how lucky I truly am. Had I been in Santa Cruz, I most likely would have been on the UCSC campus (my alma mater) or on the Mall at the Cooper House or in Book Shop Santa Cruz, all of which sustained major damage. I worked in San Leandro at the time and lived in S.F., and my normal route to and from work included the Bay Bridge and the Cypress Structure, both of which collapsed. The day after the quake I went with Butch, Hoop, Michael Beard and others on Michaels boat to check out the damage to Pier 45. We arrived just as the sun was setting. For as long as I live I will never forget the eerie site of San Francisco in total darkness save for the flashing lights of emergency vehicles and the flames from the many fires. The only other lights came from the Presidio. On Friday evening, when I finally headed back to my apartment in San Francisco, as I came down 101 approaching the Golden Gate Bridge, and once again could see the lights of The City, I literally cried with relief.

13 Greg "Grego" Dana February 13, 2009 at 02:41AM
I was at the State Park Ranger's Academy down in Asilomar (Pacific Grove) when it hit. I had just finished class and was in my room when the world started to rattle. You could pretty much see the area of the epicenter from where we were (across the bay from Monterey) but PG sits on a granite outcrop so damage was light. My roommate yelled to get in the doorway but (knowing how old those buildings were) said F--- that and bolted outside to a clearing. One of my classmates saw a deer freeze for a sec then bolt head on into a large tree killing it's self. I really started to worry when I couldn't get KGO or KCBS on the radio. I had listened to those two stations when ever I lived in the area since the early 60's and the fact that they were off the air told me that something was really wrong. They were going to swear us in as Rangers (we were about a week or 2 away from finishing P.O.S.T.) and send us to Monterey or the Bay area but didn't. If they had, my life might be totally different now as I was hurt just before graduation and terminated. If I had been a "sworn peace officer", they wouldn't have been able to do that.
