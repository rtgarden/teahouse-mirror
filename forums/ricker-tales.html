3 [2ao3o6xb6daou] February 02, 2008 at 01:41AM
Dave almost always greeted me cheerfully, with a big smile. Oh, I saw him plenty of times in his full curmudeonly splendour, but he rarely turned it on me. He was a great one for coming up with word games. My favorite Ricker word game was when he would reverse the first letter or syllable on the first and last names of the folks kicking around lot during pre-faire. Lots of people picked up on the game, and added variations. They became the standard for addressing each other. I was Vory Celtman. There was Trow Bobbridge, and couples like Seric and Arah. One of the biggest belly laughs I ever heard come out of Dave was one day at the teahouse, at a time when Spike and Daniel were building booths together -- they became Dike and Spaniel. He was particularly tickled by his own name -- Rave Dicker. I think that's why he started the game in the first place...

7 [2c9hwt6yphj6c] January 21, 2008 at 07:09AM
Okay, as billed: Brennan was maybe five or six years old - so it would've been RPF Devore around '95. It's kinda hot and the coffee house is all but deserted. It's a typical Monday afternoon: brunch is over, the Mullah has gone to the bank and to a movie and has left one of us "in charge". This day it was David. Brennan, his mom Deborah and I are sitting on straw bales just relaxing and passing the time - you know - Artist's Free Day. Maybe one or two others scattered around sitting on bales. Anyway, I'm noodling around on my six string and Bren asks me if he can go behind the counter and get a soda. I tell him it's okay with me but that he should ask Dave as he's in charge today. Now, DB is Bren's uncle and the coffee house was one of the places in which he was raised. I should also mention at this point that expletives and curses were something that Brennan didn't use - we explained to him that they had their place, like when the hammer hits the thumb or your boat sinks - but if you swore a lot you took the power out of the words. So he goes behind the counter and says, "Dave, can I go in the ice chest and get a Hansens". Dave: "NO!" Brennan: "Why not Dave?" Dave (still reading his newspaper): "Because I said so you Little Brat!" Then we hear this small even voice say "Dave?" Dave: "WHAT!" Bren: "fuck you Dave". I heard no reply, but Deb and I looked at each other in complete amazement, our jaws hitting the deck - we'd never heard him say anything like that to anyone. All I could say when he came out from behind the counter was that I'd get his soda. Which I did as I looked at Dave and just shrugged like, "what can ya do?" Later that week, when we talked about it, I told Brennan that I felt that his was an honest expression and not out of line but it would be a good idea if he were to address the incident with Dave and clear the air. Which he did the next weekend. Basically, he told Dave that he hoped that there were no hard feelings. I thought Dave handled it like a Mensch. He said that he'd deserved to be addressed thusly and if any apologies were due it was probably he that should give one to Brennan. Dave wasn't known for his affinity for kids, but more than once after that day he told me how well he thought of this kid. Just when you might think ol' Ricker was monochromatic he'd show you another side. Rosin up the bow and saw that thing old son - you're missed.

Coupla tales come to mind right now. Got time for one right now. Early "70's. Maybe the last Pumpkin Festival that was actually in Calabassas. Jack Farley, the promoter, gave me a handful of passes and encouraged me to bring some musical friends for street & stage entertainment (we could be had back then almost as cheaply as we can be now); he'd also hired Dave as some kind of musical coordinator that season. Lotta good music came out of my booth after hours at the Agoura Renfair back then - country. blues, acoustic boogie, originals and like that...that's why Jack invited us to the Festival. So things are goin' along pretty good. We're playing in the street; boothies toked up our "hat" better than most of the marks and otherwise encouraged us to hang out and play. Remember now, at the RPF we were bound, sometimes ridiculously, by someone's version of historical accuracy. Dave was, as were many of us, steeped in this, so I wasn't shocked as were my five or six friends (a bit surprised, yeah) when at the end of a tune he came up to us and asked to speak to me. Certainly discreet & respectful. Out of earshot of others he said, " Ric, you guys sound pretty good but they weren't singing in four part harmonies in the old west, do you think you could you keep it down to two?" I figured "that's just Dave" as I said Sure. My friends, however responded variously and it took a while to convince 'em that it was okay and we could just continue as we were. When I related the story to Jack, who'd hired Dave, he just laughed and said he loved Dave and what he did but, " He doesn't know what it took me a while to find out: that when people come to my show they want to party. I leave that strict historical stuff to Phyllis". When I have time I'll tell y'all about Dave's first encounter with my son Brennan (when he was 5 or 6 years old).

30 [1rw777lryxji5] December 27, 2007 at 07:34AM
When I was a young, brash fiddler, I was hanging out with Steeger and others during a folk festival in San Diego. The Highwoods Stringband was there, and Steeger offered some "Damage de Kookoo" to Walt Koken in order to soften him up to get him to play a tune into my tape recorder. Brian wanted him to play "Mineola Rag", which was a great new tune Highwoods had been playing. We went off to the Greek theater and sat in the bleachers smoking. Another fellow joined us. He had a banjo, a leather hat (!) and a big, leather-bound can of Bugler pipe tobacco hanging from his belt. We were getting along fine and Brian suggested Walt play that tune into my "time machine". Walt said: Sure thing, that's a great tune. Just as he was about to put the bow to the strings, Ricker ( Yes, it was he ) said: "Better not play that one for them, that's a pretty good tune!" Walt looked at him uncertainly and paused. Then he said: "OK, Dave". That sort of elitism was completely against why anyone even goes to a festival like that!!! That's how I became acquainted with Dave Ricker. Later, when Dave was off somewhere else, Walt came by where Brian and I were sitting in the Lobby and played the tune next to us without saying a thing. We turned on the recorder and got it.

I bought Ricker's trailer on Snob Hill, parked permanently under a huge Madrone tree between Bob McGowan's and the K-Pat trailer that became Bud Coffee's at one point. I paid Ricker $200 in cash for it, which I thought was an amazing steal since my tent at Jungleland in Agoura cost that and was only a tent, right? Anyway, when I moved into it...it had one wall covered with carefully decoupaged beer labels...and it was done so carefully and with such reverence that I had to leave the wall as it was. Aside from that one Rickerian feature, it was remarkably clean and I didn't have to disinfect it, as many suggested I would have to. Dave was always extraordinarily kind to me (not sure why). I lived in the trailer for 3 years I think, then sold it to Dwight Lloyd for Twinkie to live in.
