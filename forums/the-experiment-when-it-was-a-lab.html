3 Murray then, now aka MAX January 22, 2009 at 02:28PM
The green umbrell a was exlplained to me in great detail by Dennis Day. His own court character newington butts had one of the more ingenious green umbrellas: hw would wander around with the end of his long pointed velvet cap in his goblet of ale. This prop managed to do several things at once for him, it confirmed that the stumbling step and haltering speech was from spirits, it showed he was deep in his cups, it gave people an immediately accessible point of interaction "Sir your hat has fallen in your cup"....Newington removes, or customer removes, and somehow within minutes it is right back in there. Dennis and Peg insisted that every character had to find a way to have their own green umbrella. My first couple years on the streets I had a prop letter, which I would wander around having people read to me. It was from a sailor I had befriended who actually has me married to him by proxy. It wasnt a very good green umbrella but as a gig it worked nicely.

8 [xj4gqw5wi0c2] January 18, 2009 at 12:58AM
Great topic, I spent a lot of time talking about this stuff with Peg. Just off the top of my head: 1. Go for historical detail everywhere, but don't let it go to your head, good scholarship should be good fun 2. Living history is NOT really an attempt to replicate like a museum, in fact it updates ageless traditions and ritual to the modern age, which is course what all good tradition and ritual always has. Only then is it entertaining, and that's what we were paid to do, entertain (indirectly sell beer and lots of it) 3. Balance scripted material with intentional space for improvisation with others, especially the audience. Think this is almost the most technique. (Like, there would be a moment in dance where we'd pre-planned to let chaos take over and we all pile off stage and dive into the crowd and do what ever occurs to us, for about 30 seconds, and then run back up on stage and return to script. In other words, keep busting through the fourth wall, proscenium mentality of an observing audience so conditioned to watching TV or movies passively. 4. Find your "green umbrella" and let it take you to a special place, and bring you back to character when the spirit is not quite there and you start "phoning it in." Something physical to hold onto, part of the costume, a hat, a cane, a special scarf 5. Anyone who can't drop character offstage, is never going to really have it onstage. Used to drive me nuts after hours at night, people wandering around in accent, garb and clutching to character. Really sad, looked like jerks. 6. Well planned and placed off-stage/street play can drive customers in droves to ale stands, increasing profits and justifying performer's wages All in all, it is "Environmental Theater" ... being able to respond in the moment improvisational, depart from script when the environment creates a sudden condition for you, sun parts through the crowds, the rain starts, fry an egg in a skillet on stage, take the conversation off stage to a customer sitting on a hay bale and interact with them directly. I could go on all day ...

11 Murray then, now aka MAX January 11, 2009 at 05:44PM
yes part of it was the idea of "everymans history" which was way way ahead of its time. The idea that the lives of regular people were an important way of looking at history. More than justthe lives of the decorated and pampered folk mattered. Bob was really big on conserving and revitalizing all the amazing tech from the era too. Draw bridges and water powered mills. He had a foot pedal lathe and he and wood worker don used to talk for hours about how much they admired men who could go into the woods with a package of tools, and make a hootch and live and carefully harvest wood in a clean way and just live lightly in the woods making things. this view of history, and eventually "Herstory" also, spread far and wide in the world because it was delivered with the great "sugar coated pill" of tits and beer. The fun drew you in and the historicity of it all got you thinking. Bob was also really into the idea of Celtic power. He used to raise his fist at the spool table often and say "Celtic pride ...Celtic power" shaking it in the air and shouting "CELTIC POWER". and then very sober quietly as if an aside "Someday they will say that you know". and they do. we have pride now

14 Eric Lethe January 11, 2009 at 04:36PM
Well of course there was the formula for street interaction: 1. Interact directly in the street with at least five customers every hour 2. Interact with them as though they are visiting your century 3. You live in the 16th century, in a small village in England 4. Never drop character except backstage Remember all the classes taught in workshops? When we were establishing minimum requirements, Meet and Greet was considered one of three most important skill to acquire (along with BFA and Character). All of this to create an illusion of traveling to another time.
