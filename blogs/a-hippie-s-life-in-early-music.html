5 Carrie Smith March 25, 2009 at 06:46AM
Morgan, I have always wanted to be a musician. And while I taught myself to read music and tried on a few different instruments in an attempt to aspire to the virtuosity of my parents, I was horrible. The music I could make, the rhythm I could *feel* I couldn't make come out of the instrument. Until I learned that my instrument was my body, that I was a dancer. I could view a dance or a step once, and like a musician who is just hearing a tune at a session for the first time, jump in the second go around. As a dancer, I get a lot from the musicians who play while I perform. I have danced to taped music and to musicians who were just "calling it in" and my dancing, while still entertaining to the audience, lacked a certain something. But when I dance to a "real" musician, someone whose soul sings out through the music they make, my dancing changes. The steps, the figures, the movement become secondary. The music takes my body and my soul is dancing with the soul they have sent out. It is the embodiment of pure joy. You are that kind of musician, Morgan. Even now, when I no longer dance, I still hear your music and my soul starts to sing. Thank you for that.

11 Greg "Grego" Dana March 02, 2009 at 04:36AM
Touched a nerve. This has been one of my tender points through the years. I don't even read notation fluently. So I rejoice every time I hear about famous musos who don't read, and reassure myself with notions of a tradition of spirit being as valid as one of academia. The movie "Songcatcher" deals nicely with that. We see academics who have great lists of folk songs, but aint got no soul. Then there are the mountain hicks of Appalachia, who carry the heart of music in the bosom of their illiterate culture and can melt you with a riff. That wouldn't be a riff on a fancy axe either. Real folk tradition is about making due with what you have. Kenny Millikin could inspire me more with his piss-can rumblepot that most people who bang away on a Steinway. But then, like you, the dark side takes hold again. I remember Mitchel, Dorn, Sue, Sherry, how some people actually have soul and education. Scary. So awesome you almost think they could fart in micro-tonal harmony. Even lowly creatures like drummers; boy, some of the famous people Dave Miles has recorded with... So I remember not to try to justify ignoring my neurosis with rationalizations that attempt to lift myself up by putting other people down. Academics do great work digging up and revealing long lost material, interpreting. Musical wizards actually create new stuff out of thin air. Charmers have an uncanny knack for "sucking your heart out through a soda straw", however simple or complex the music. Some rock, some roll. We're all different. The ultimate value of any of that to performers like us winds up being reflected in how the music moves us as we play it, and how it touches those who hear it. On a totally personal note (ahem), I've gotta say that a sure recipe for bullying myself into depression is for me to compare my accomplishments with those of other people. Wind up feeling like a bug hovering over a pile of my own stinky poo. But did you know that mosquitoes don't fight over potential mates like so many animals do, but rather bring their wing vibration sounds into tune to get horny over harmonious vibes? And, gotta add, Morgan, that you are one of those great musicians that I've always been a little shy around. My first memory of you was in troll hollow, where you and a girlfriend were sitting in your tent after hours singing, holding court. Gathered around the door to that tent were bunches of people drinking in the sound of your voices as if their ears had an unquenchable case of cotton mouth. One of the adoring masses was me. Maybe some day those of massive accomplishments will study us lowlifes and find value in our primitive, naive, simple musings. Or maybe some day I'll stop thinking validation of my stuff should come from people with stinkin' badges. Meanwhile, I'll probably keep wondering why my sheets are so begging for a laundering every time I forget to remember the blessing of the goddess that caused the stains.
