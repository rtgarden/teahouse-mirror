1 [3jiiarjwczptn] April 09, 2009 at 05:16AM
San Juan Bautista Ball canceled for 2009 I wrote the State Park officals and volunteers last night (my introduction to this gig was through Frank Davis and Brassworks. Innitially we just transplanted our Fezziwig's routines, then Frank I added more and more Old California music and dances. I have worked with three different bands over the years there: Like you, I am quite saddened at the news of the cancellation of the San Juan Bautista Ball this year. I sincerely hope for the return of this charming event in 2010. I understand the economic realities of today. I thank you for your kind words to me. I am grateful to you and all of the other volunteers through the years who have worked so hard to maintain this jewel of an event. I want you to know that the San Juan Bautista Ball has served to preserve and promote Spanish California music and dance and contributed to a revival of interest in this culture. The State Park is a very, very special place and The Plaza History Association is comprised of many special people. Over the years, interested dancers and musicians from all over California have attended and sang the praises of The San Juan Bautista Ball. I have a personal history with the Ball going back to the 1980’s. I remember, I had to find a substitute Dancemaster in 1989 because my wife, Marilyn, had just delivered our older son, Robert, a week before. Robert will be twenty this year. The Ball became an annual family tradition for the Letchworths. Marilyn joined me and added her unique sparkle to the evening’s festivities. Our boys, year after year, attended the day’s events with us. They learned a firsthand history of the town, the mission and the park. They met the re-enactors who eagerly shared their enthusiasm for San Juan. Our sons, when young camped with the Rendezvousers and eventually joined the ball. Robert became an enthusiastic dancer (especially when he discovered young ladies outnumbered the young men 3.5 to 1). Our younger son, Tommy, delighted his father when he became a fiddler in the band the last two years. I never made much profit, feeding and housing a family of four for the weekend, but it became a sort of working vacation for me, every Father’s Day. I never did it for the money, though the money made it happen. Old California music and dance continues in other venues. Please let dancers know I am leading an Old California Dance workshop at the San Francisco Free Folk Festival on June 13/14th 2009. I do not know which day yet. Check the website at www.sffolkfest.org. There will also be a Miner’s Ball in Placerville featuring Old California dance and the band “Legacy” on Saturday June 27, 2006 sponsored by the Shakespeare Club. Feel free to contact me for info for info. The pleasure has been mine. Thank You, Jim Letchworth

I posted a couple of recent photos of Los Calicantos, one of the Old California groups I work with. I met them through the Olompoli State Park gig Phyllis set up last year. It was another one of those "artistic successes". It really was a wonderful chance to network and these guys are a connection I made. The current director, Lance Beesom is desended from many of the families who made the trek up from Mexico with de Anza. My friend, Rose Berryessa is another, she has done a few fairs too. The group sings traditional songs with great harmonies, most also play and I am the Tecolero or Dancemaster. One of the photos shows a girls soccer team who joined in at Aviso adobe in Pleasenton. The band is my son Tommy on fiddle, son Robert on bass and friend Gavin Valasquez-Murray on guitar. It was a real barn dance. Tommy will join me for some of the Los Calicantos gigs. 10 [1xvdtzq634x8] February 19, 2009 at 03:14PM
Jay, bless his heart, was prone to a blood and thunder approach, both off and on the boards. You'd hear bombs going off in Court/Glade and know that Mr. W was at it again. It was all sort of the "manly men bellow" thing. Which was odd because it was always the other gay boys who were shouting; the straight guys tended to be pretty calm. Unfortunately what often would have best been handled with a whisper (from a logistics standpoint) was often dragged out, full voice, and an attempt was made to make it a "gig." Yelling at servants and groveling was another variantion on this theme which I absolutely hated. Court in the 1970s was pretty much universally detested on sight (for a variety of reasons, most of which had to do with how we were dressed rather than how we acted per se) and I recall doing a tremendous amount of pro-active fence mending (waiting on folks backstage, holding doors, being extra considerate, helping out where I could) just to try to offset this. Interesting thread.
