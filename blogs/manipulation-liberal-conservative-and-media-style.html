9 Danese Cooper May 02, 2009 at 07:21AM
Cat, in response to your question, I honestly don't know. I see a couple of huge issues. One of which is the mind is like a muscle. It must be exercised to stay in shape. Unfortunately a lot of people believe that because they went to college their minds are still in shape. My response is back in the day, I could party and screw all night then get up and work all day. That was then, this is now. Just because someone went to college doesn't mean that they haven't gotten mentally flabby. A friend and I were discussing this issue and he came up with the following analogy. Every car has a blind spot when you are driving it. You can't see into it unless you physically change your position. Yet how many drivers really bother to do that? In the same vein, how many people really step outside of their social circle, lifestyle and perspective? OR, and this is important, how often do people tell themselves that they do? It's easy to tell yourself that you are informed by sound bites and blogs, but are you really? How many people have actually researched a subject in depth (and from all sides) vs. just reading a blog and op/ed piece or two and consider themselves informed? The second issue is something that my wife and disagree on. I am of the opinion that with the decay of journalistic standards people have lost the ability to tell news from op/ed from blogging from propaganda from entertainment. My wife believes that the majority of people can tell the difference. If hadn't met met people who tell me that Rush Limbaugh and Michael Moore 'tell the truth' while mainstream news sources are lying, I'd have more faith. Realistically, I think that the shifting of journalism from reporting to commenting, interjecting opinion and spicing up the news was in response to the pressure of competing with the entertainment industry. In doing so though, they began to undermine their own credibility. In the same vein, I'm pretty much of the opinion that the news media shot themselves in the foot by editorializing about the news to the point that they opened the door to blogging. What happened is the electronic pundit and internet blogger eclipsed legitimate journalism because they could produce and distribute pieces faster than stories that had to be researched and checked. So I really don't know what the future of journalism is going to be. I do know that we just had a major newspaper fold here in Denver. It will be interesting.
