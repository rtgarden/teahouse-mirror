Begin forward from Geri: "Hi Louisa, Chipping –under-Oakwood! Talk about a blast from the past! Where was Chipping –under-Oakwood? There is a spot on modern map of England where the counties of Oxfordshire, Wiltshire and Royal Barkshire meet. If however, you are fortunate enough to find a copy of a very old map printed in a sometime-somewhen, and you look very, very carefully, and if you are not a doubting-Thomas, you may be able to see that the borders of each shire have been pushed back just a bit and in the space this creates, there sits the wee community of Chipping-under-Oakwood. The Thames River is nearby and it is just off The Great Foss Way, which was a major rout from London to the major West Country communities of Bristol, and onwards to Exeter and Plymouth. From north to south it sat just off the main rout from London to Oxford, Banbury, Stratford and on to York, New Castle on Tine and beyond, the wilds of the barbarous Scots. Although Lizzy Tudor traveled extensively, she never in her life was further than 50 miles from London, despite Hollywood. The Oakwood part for the Northern Faire in Novato goes without saying. I wanted to come up with a different name for Southern Faire but Phyllis vetoed it. 14 PatriciaBlanco February 01, 2011 at 10:52PM
Okay youse guys: Noel pulled me away from the FaceBook vortex and I thank him greatly for that lifeline tug.  For the record: the name, Chipping-Under-Oakwood, was invented and promoted by Geraldine Duncann.  She had traveled around England quite a bit by the mid-1970's, and was impressed by the similarity of the woodland oak forests around Oxford, and surmised that our Faire Village (hitherto as yet un-named, unless referred to as The Shire, by Tolkien fans), was probably in a similar place, metaphorically speaking.  The official naming of the village market town coincided with the push to create a more authentic history-time-travel experience for our patrons.  This movement included the move to a more 'authentic' dialect (introduced by Robert Easton before RPFS75), more authentic costuming for the villagers and craftsmen (on-going, actually, from the early 1970's), and Phyllis's desire to promote the Living History Center as an educational outlet for both schools (Workshop-in-the-Woods) and community outreach. Since Geraldine is one of my friends on FaceBook, I'm going to ask her to print her timeline and reasonings for the specific name "Chipping-Under-Oakwood."  IIRC, she did an entire pre-Faire workshop on the premise of CUO beginning in at least spring 1976, and continuing through at least RPFN77. More to follow (when / if Geraldine has time to add to this conversation) PS - I'll invite her to the teahouse, too, of course.
