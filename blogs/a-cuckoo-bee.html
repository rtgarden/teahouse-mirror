A cuckoo bee
By Murray then, now aka MAX | May 09, 2008 | 0 comments
Right the first of May I knew spring was really on us because I saw my first cuckoo bee. It was concerned by my nearness for only half a moment and then it settled into choosing between the different blossoms on the porch. Would it choose pansies? Or nasturtiums? Interesting to me it was very intent on the blossoms of the jade plant in the blue shelves with the other succulents. Little tiny white blossoms decorated with a very intent cuckoo bee, which looks like a medium sized bumble bee with a long mouth like a hummingbird. Today I made the fence up on the top garden by using up the long section of metal fence that I salvaged at Blackpoint. There is a peculiar delight in using up the stray bits you have salvaged for the purpose in the yard. This fence is made of a lot of that effort, old bits of fence and bamboo supports and now the fence all woven in in a sort of punk rock inspired by zen gardens type thing. The deer here are really sweet and they practically come right in the house, this means that the last number of years they have just come when the little tomatoes are forming and just eaten them right off of the bush. Seriously I haven't had a really full blown garden since I moved back, which was around the end of Blackpoint Renaissance Faire (10 years!)... and that says something about how long it takes to get things done in the yard sometimes. The main project for some time has been getting the medium front greenhouse dug in, re-built, covered, and then clad on the sides. Everything has a sort of daring do in it because the last year I really didn't do much in the yard (I have been recovering from a head injury) and so I just have to bluster past how much hasn't been done and delight in what little I can do right now. So I used up the happy little stretch of fence, which has good memories because I salvaged it with Bevan and it was a gesture of early friendship that really gave us a little history. I used to use this same section of fence on the other garden which was dug out to put in the front greenhouse.When all the dirt was dug out for the greenhouse which is partially dug into the hill it created a lovely border garden around the edge of the greenhouse which has a lot of sage and about a dozen artichokes in various stages of growth. Deer do not really seem to eat the artichokes and they are starting to get really big. In the morning of the same day that I saw the cuckoo bee I saw my first swallows. They were soaring and dipping along the sand bluffs between Redway and Garberville. As soon as I saw them I thought how interesting that the first place they are starting to nest is actually a natural spot. The bluffs are very high maybe half a mile to a mile tall with a good size bit carved out to make the road. They are brownish with a bit of gold and red and sometimes big chunks of sandstone fall from them onto the road, like mostly around New Years at syzygee and perigee when the planetary bodies all get close (or the sun the moon and the earth) and all hell breaks loose here and it just pours water from the sky and the rivers rise and the roads close and you can just set your clock by it. Thats one of the reasons why I love living in California; there are all sorts of surprises you can rely on. Today I also trimmed all the suckers off of my pomegranate tree. It was all part of working the last third of the fence up, the corner where the fence was being started up again , which is kind of wedge shaped, has the "wonderful" pomegranate in it. It is a grafter tree, so the rootstock wants to just choke out the young tree which is really curvy and getting sorta tall and treelike and actually producing fruit. Its good news for that pomegranate to be pruned at the base because there were lots of suckers, and it is just the last couple years been getting its roots down into the water deep under the soil. You can always tell when a tree really connects to the groundwater, they get this vibrant burst of growth, and often that includes a burst of surprisingly large suckers. As I was digging in the transplanted fava beans and tomatoes and peppers, I was excited to see how really rich the soil is. It was a good confirmation that it has been the deer holding back that garden not the quality of the soil. This garden is sort of historic in that it was one of the first organic gardens here created 35 years ago by some of the same people who went on to found the popular local seed company "Seeds of Change".Once I start watering it all in I am bound to find many volunteer mizuna mustards and some catnip that tend to volunteer. I encourage them. The pomegranate tree was planted around the same time that I salvaged the fence, so it was good to take care of them and use the bit of fencing up and I even had that exciting "aha!" where the bit of salvaged fencing was the perfect length to complete the fence around the garden. I then used some of the flexible black square mesh stuff to make a simple gate, with a latch. Over the years I have learned that the fence is no good, even if you make a gate if the gate is left without a latch. Because the deer are smart and they know every bit of food in the yard i must guard the sorrel. They eat things right off of the porch so that I have had to hang the flowers from hanging pots because they don't seem to be tall enough to get at them that way. Now I understand that rancher fashion of having hanging baskets of flowers all around the porch. A good start to the season overall. It is a small garden and yet it can be quite prolific with peppers and ichiban eggplants and okra coming on very strong at the end of the season. In great part the entire metaphor, the trimming of my tee of life, the creation of a garden from an old garden and bits of fence, it is all a mirror to how much my life has changed since this teahouse began pouring out miracles. Can it be that after getting knitted up back together over the years with my tribe and after creating a little corner for us to play in that I am starting to get used to myself? Or maybe together with everyone else I am just more myself than ever. Thank you. You have inspired me to plant a garden.
