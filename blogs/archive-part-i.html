Wed, December 20, 2006 - 11:51 AM So we finally got in to see the ped on Monday to talk to her about M's lack of speech. She's referred us to Children's Hospital for a pure tone hearing test and for a pediatric neurology consult. That's set up for a month from now. Initially, the whole visit freaked me out and made me want to cry. I had really been naively hoping that she would just tell me he's fine and we've got nothing to worry about. M showed off some of his signs and completed basic tasks for her, let her look in his ear, and was generally a cute kid. I was worried because he had fallen asleep in his carseat on the way there and is usually clingy and uncommunicative when he's just woken up but he did better than normal in that regard. After talking a bit, she called in her attending and he said something about P2 (which I've only been able to correlate to Piaget's stages of cognitive development) all the while I sat just feeling rather numb to the whole thing. M on the other hand was showing signs of distress because we were in a small room with stuff he wasn't allowed to touch. I could also sense that he was a bit dismayed that everyone was talking about him in the third person and that he couldn't also talk to us. I left the appointment feeling weak, sad, and as if I have failed him as a parent. Yesterday, I posted in a forum to see if anyone had feedback for me on the visit. I was feeling rather helpless and without knowledge on the subject and, by this point, googling was causing more anxiety than comfort. Someone mentioned apraxia which I promptly googled only to begin feeling more anxious. I know M hasn't been diagnosed with it but many of the symptoms really ring true with what we have been seeing in him. Then again, if you look and try hard enough you can convince yourself of anything. Nevertheless, I have always strongly believed in diet affecting behavior and development so I googled "apraxia, changes in diet" and came across a site for CHERAB where I read some really great stuff. Ok, it's not so great that kids suffer from this but the fact that others have dealt with it before me with success is great. Regardless of my armchair diagnosis, I read some extremely promising stories of parents who have begun supplementing with DHA, EPA and GLA fatty acids. So many success stories of results within weeks sent me out to the store this morning where I bought two different supplements for kids. He's asleep right now but you can bet I'm excited for him to wake up. More to follow soon...
