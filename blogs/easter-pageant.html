EASTER PAGEANT
By Murray then, now aka MAX | September 10, 2017 | 0 comments
Luisa Puig March 27, 2016 ·  I have many fond memories of Easter at the Southern Faire site, usually on either Workshop Weekends, or Dress Rehearsal. The most memoriable, though, was from RPFS'1973 when Easter Sunday actually fell on an Opening Weekend! Since it could not be ignored, Entertainment was told to highlight it by using the Folk Pageants of the Midieval and Renaissance traditions. John Green Tree Murphy Well, as I recall, that was the year the Entertainment Department mounted a Medieval Pageant, only instead of wagons going from place to place, Christ and His attendants walked from station to station followed by the crowd. At each station was enacted a scene from the Passion. Theryl and I were at the final station... the Hell Mouth. I played Satan and Theryl played Beeelzebub. In that scene, Christ broke the locks on the Gates of Hell and redeemed the prophets. As usual, I had to be ready for whatever improvisation might catch Theryl's fancy... we had a scene to ourselves before Christ showed up... At one point Theryl talked me into lighting and smoking a crook cigar. He provided me with a LOOOONG match to light it with... My costume was all gauze and when I struck the match I set myself on fire. Luckily I was able to extinguish it before it caught hold of the mass of fabric... I did not do that again... that was the origin of the Hell Mouth set piece that was forever after hauled from fair to fair but never used again, to my knowledge. I never did get to see the other scenes, but that may be where your memory of Ginger originates... I was not in Faust and never saw it, I was not a member of PDA. Ray Tatar Thank you, your Majestie!  Here is a story of that fateful Easter in 1973 when we open the Faire on Easter weekend. In getting the Paramount Ranch site approved for the Faire, one day it was decreed by the powers that be in Los Angeles County that we had to open on an Easter weekend. While we all groaned about it, Phyllis Patterson, who never saw such things as a deterrent, asked me to find a passion play or some chronicle to present that depicted the Rising of Christ from the tomb. I shuddered at that challenge because I knew the Wakefield plays and other English plays to be really boring. But Ron Patterson suggested that I approach the researcher on Theme Events staff to help me find a piece that I liked. I asked her to find a passion play that was interesting and maybe had some humor in it.  To my surprise, she located a play called the “Redentin Easter Play”. It was an obscure medieval German play. I thought that the script she picked covered the story of the event making the Romans as buffoons and leaving the actual rising as a religious event. When I began to read it, I laughed at the opening scene in Pontius Pilate’s court. It read like a Marx brother’s comedy featuring the comic antics of four Roman guards that Pilate sends to the guard the tomb of Jesus from grave robbers. The actors who got those roles were screamingly funny. I knew that with the original funny lines and the improvisational abilities of Faire actors, this would be a success. I cast a guy (Bob Strnad) as Christ. I cast Strnad solely because of his astounding resemblance to Christ, but I had to cut most of his lines because his voice at that time had a youthful squeak which would not work! When the Angel of God (Doug Broyles) who had a voice like thunder, opened the tomb, the four comic guards made an uproarious exit.  Now Broyles was one of those actors that spewed tiny bits of saliva as he spoke. He had a long speech over the open tomb and when he ended, he would say: “Arise, man of God!” This spewing annoyed Strnad so much so, that he pulled his long tunic up over his face. Just as he did, Broyles glanced down into the tomb and said: “Arise man of G-A-W-D! Strnad had been naked under his tunic. Luckily, the 200 or so people watching this happen could not see Strnad in the tomb. When he rose up in the tomb and proclaimed: “Resurrecti Alleluia!” his tunic was back in place. The audience was moved by Strnad’s Christlike face—gasps and wide eyes flashed through the crowd. Phyllis liked the piece and only made one suggestion. There was a tree over the tomb and she thought a flute sound from someone in the tree would embellish the scene as Christ rose. I did too, and it worked like a charm. I had the tomb set between the Drury Creek Theater (Pilates Court) and the Main stage (Hell). As the audience moved from there to the tomb, pitchmen dressed as Angels led them in a mock religious procession. One of them was the tall bearded Dundee who carried a large calligraphic angel on a pole while he intoned: “This way to the TOMB OF JESUS!”… “There is a seat for each and every one of you near the TOMB OF JESUS”.  After the rising, Angels blew their post horns followed by Dundee’s imploring: “Follow me to the Mouth of Hell… “There is a seat for each and every one of you near the MOUTH OF HELL!”. The crowd followed him to the main stage were Steve Gillan (Chief) had designed and erected a huge ten foot hell mouth. The eye sockets of the hell head had holes large enough for the heads of Satan and Lucifer. I cannot remember who played these two characters, but they were terrific. They saw that Christ is coming to open the mouth of hell and are really angry. Angels with drums and cymbals heighted the approach. The two devils began to toss Elizabethan imprecations at the Angel of God as he led the procession of Christ and the attendant angels. The two devils had to improvise to cover while the angels were getting the audience fully seated on the hay bales. They of course tried to get audience members to join them in their fight against Christ. Once on the stage the Angel of God waved a fiery sword at the devils and they backed off. Soon the devils began to argue that the souls Christ wanted to free from hell, they had won fair and square. This went on for a while but the fiery sword won the day. The devils finally got out of the way cursing: “We will be baaaack.. just you wait Jesus, just you wait!”.  When Christ opened the hell mouth with a wave of his hand, Adam (Stefan Tatar) and Eve (Nancy Hughes) were the first to come out. They were followed by John the Baptist (???) and a host of other old and new testament cross-over’s. We had a ladder set on the stage against the railing of the upper walk on the backstage wall. There was also a painted cloud fastened up on the upper walk for the actors to disappear into. All the characters from hell climbed into the cloud with Jesus waving goodby to a jubilant audience.  At the first performance, a young boy shouted as Christ was heading into the cloud: “Save a place up there for me Jesus! Phyllis and Ron liked the show so well that they had it run every Sunday for the rest of the Faire. John Green Tree Murphy More about the Hell Mouth... a couple of years later, at 3rd Dickens, the Hell Mouth was stored in the back by the office. I was electrician and was not provided with an assistant, so I was working extremely long hours. I slept on site and it was very cold in the cavernous warehouse. I grabbed a 55 gallon garbage bag and crawled into the Hell Mouth... it was at least a small enclosure that would hold some heat... and used the garbage bag for a sleeping bag. Worked great! Next morning I was talking to Woz, who had been on guard duty overnight. He told me that the Hell Mouth was haunted; he had heard a growling noise coming from it all night long... I forgot to mention that I snore...
